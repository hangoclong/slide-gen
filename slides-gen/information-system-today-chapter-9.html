Preview: The Breadth of Systems

Information systems are not just software. When we refer to "systems," we are talking about a broad range of technologies:

  • Hardware: Servers, computers, mobile devices.
  • Software: Operating systems, business applications, mobile apps.
  • Services: Cloud computing, consulting, support.

Regardless of your major (Marketing, Finance, Management), you will be involved in systems development or acquisition. Understanding these processes is critical for your future success.

Context: The Maker Movement

Innovation is no longer limited to large corporations. The "Maker Movement" represents a shift towards do-it-yourself (DIY) and do-it-with-others (DIWO) innovation.

Cheap Computing

Devices like the Raspberry Pi Zero ($5) make processing power accessible to everyone.

Rapid Prototyping

3D printers allow individuals to manufacture physical parts from digital designs.

Open Source

Shared software and hardware designs accelerate learning and development.

Part 1: Making the Business Case

Justifying Technology Investments

Making the Business Case

Definition

The process of identifying, quantifying, and presenting the value provided by a system.

Just as a lawyer builds a case to prove innocence, a manager must build a case to prove that an IT investment adds value.

  • Proposed Systems: Used to determine a "go" or "no-go" decision.
  • Existing Systems: Used to determine whether to continue funding or decommission.

The Productivity Paradox

Despite massive investments in IT (trillions of dollars worldwide), productivity gains are not always easy to see. This phenomenon is known as the Productivity Paradox.

Why has it been difficult to show that these vast expenditures have led to productivity gains?

Factor 1: Measurement Problems

Organizations often measure the wrong things.

Efficiency

Doing things faster or cheaper. (Easier to measure, but not always the goal).

Effectiveness

Doing the right things well. (Harder to measure, but often where the real value lies).

Example: Online banking might reduce branch visits (efficiency) but its real value is customer retention (effectiveness), which is harder to quantify directly.

Factors 2 & 3: Time Lags & Redistribution

Time Lags

There can be a significant gap between when an investment is made and when the benefits are felt. It may take years for a new system to pay off.

Redistribution

New innovations may simply redistribute the "pie" rather than making it bigger. If everyone adopts a technology, market share might simply shift between competitors without increasing total industry output.

Factor 4: Mismanagement

The new system may simply have been implemented or managed poorly.

  • Bad Business Models: A good information system cannot save a bad business model.
  • Process Issues: Automating a broken process just results in a faster broken process.

Success requires a joint technology/process solution, not just a technology fix.

Three Types of Arguments

Successful business cases are often based on a combination of three types of arguments:

graph TD A[Business Case] --> B[Faith]; A --> C[Fear]; A --> D[Facts]; B --> E[Strategy & Beliefs]; C --> F[Competition & Survival]; D --> G[Data & Analysis]; style A fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5,stroke-width:2px style B fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style C fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style D fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9

Arguments Based on Faith

Based on beliefs about strategy, competitive advantage, and industry forces.

"I believe this CRM system is necessary to achieve our goal of being customer-centric."

Arguments Based on Fear

Based on the notion that the firm will lose out or go out of business if the system is not implemented.

"If we don't implement a mobile app, our competitors will crush us."

Arguments Based on Facts

Most executives prefer arguments based on data and quantitative analysis. This typically involves a detailed Cost-Benefit Analysis.

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)

Understanding not just the acquisition cost, but all costs associated with ongoing use and maintenance.

  • Non-recurring costs: One-time costs (acquisition, site prep).
  • Recurring costs: Ongoing costs (salaries, utilities, maintenance).
graph TD A[Benefits] --> B[Tangible]; A --> C[Intangible]; B --> D[Cost Savings]; B --> E[Increased Sales]; C --> F[Customer Service]; C --> G[Perception]; style A fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5 style B fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style C fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9

Tangible vs. Intangible Benefits

Tangible Benefits: Easy to quantify.
Example: Reducing error rates by 50% saves $10,000 per year in labor.

Intangible Benefits: Hard to quantify but real.
Example: Improved customer perception, better employee morale, or faster decision making.

Even intangible benefits should be tied to monetary terms whenever possible (proxy variables).

Cost-Benefit Analysis Techniques

Once costs and benefits are estimated, financial analysis determines if the project makes sense.

Break-Even Analysis

Identifies at what point tangible benefits equal tangible costs.

Net Present Value (NPV)

Calculates the relevant cash flow streams associated with the system at the organization's discount rate (time value of money).

Weighted Multicriteria Analysis

Used when deciding among different IS investments or alternative designs. It accounts for requirements and constraints that are not easily quantified in dollars.

  1. Define Requirements and Constraints.
  2. Assign Weights based on importance (e.g., Security = 18%, Cost = 15%).
  3. Rate each alternative (1-5 scale).
  4. Calculate weighted scores to find the best fit.

Knowledge Check

Which type of business case argument is based on the belief that the system is necessary to achieve the organization's strategy?

  • A) Fact
  • B) Fear
  • C) Faith
  • D) Fiction

Correct Answer: C) Faith

Arguments based on faith often hold that an information system must be implemented to achieve the organization's strategy effectively and to gain or sustain a competitive advantage, even if hard data is lacking.

Part 2: The Systems Development Process

Building the Solution

Systems Analysis and Design

The process of designing, building, and maintaining information systems.

The Systems Analyst

A professional who studies the problems and needs of an organization to determine how people, methods, and information technology can best be combined to bring about improvements.

They are not just "techies"; they need a blend of technical, managerial, and interpersonal skills.

graph TD A[Software] --> B[Custom]; A --> C[Off-the-Shelf]; B --> D[Tailored]; B --> E[Specific]; C --> F[Cheaper]; C --> G[Faster]; style A fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5 style B fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style C fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9

Custom vs. Off-the-Shelf

Custom Software (Tailor-made)

Developed to meet specific specifications.
Pros: Customizability, Problem Specificity.
Cons: Expensive, slow, risky.

Off-the-Shelf Software (Packaged)

Used to support common business processes.
Pros: Less costly, faster to procure, higher quality, less risky.
Cons: May not fit unique needs.

Open Source Software

A philosophy that promotes access to the source code of a product.

  • Free to use and modify: Often developed by a community.
  • Examples: Linux (OS), Android (Mobile), Apache (Web Server), Firefox (Browser).
  • Benefits: Stability, low cost, flexibility.
  • Risks: "Hidden" costs of support and training.

The Systems Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

Describes the life of an information system from conception to retirement. It is a structured, disciplined approach.

Phase 1: Systems Planning and Selection

Organizations have limited resources, so they must select projects that yield the most benefit.

  • Goal: Identify, plan, and select a development project.
  • Activities: Building the business case, assessing feasibility, and prioritizing projects.
  • Outcome: A decision to accept or reject a project.

Phase 2: Systems Analysis

Designers gain a thorough understanding of the current way of doing things and determine what is needed from the new system.

Requirements Collection

The process of gathering information from users, managers, and documents. "Garbage in, garbage out" applies here.

Techniques:

  • Interviews & Questionnaires
  • Observations & Document Analysis
  • Joint Application Design (JAD)

Modeling Data and Processes

Analysts use tools to organize requirements.

Data Modeling

Describing the data needed (e.g., Entity-Relationship Diagrams). Shows entities like "Student", "Class", and their relationships.

Process Modeling

Describing how data moves and is transformed (e.g., Data Flow Diagrams, Pseudocode). Represents the logic of the system.

Phase 3: Systems Design

The details of the chosen approach are elaborated. The "How" is defined.

  • Processing and Logic: Specifying algorithms and rules.
  • Databases and Files: Creating the actual database schema.
  • Human-Computer Interface (HCI): Designing forms, reports, and the user interface (UI) for usability.

Phase 4: Systems Implementation

Transforming the design into a working system.

  • Programming: Coding the software.
  • Testing:
    • Developmental: Testing modules.
    • Alpha: Testing overall system internally.
    • Beta: Testing with actual users.

System Conversion Strategies

graph TD A[Parallel] --> B[Old & New run together]; C[Direct] --> D[Old stops, New starts]; E[Phased] --> F[Modules introduced slowly]; G[Pilot] --> H[One location first]; style A fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5 style C fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5 style E fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5 style G fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5

Parallel is safest but expensive. Direct is cheapest but riskiest.

Training and Support

Even the best system will fail if users don't know how to use it.

Documentation

User guides, reference manuals, and tutorials.

Training

Classes, one-on-one instruction, or computer-aided instruction.

Support

Help desks and ongoing problem-solving assistance.

Systems Maintenance

The largest part of system development effort often occurs after implementation.

  • Corrective: Repairing flaws and bugs.
  • Adaptive: Evolving functionality for changing business needs.
  • Preventive: Reducing chance of future failure.
  • Perfective: Enhancements and "bells and whistles".

Knowledge Check

Which phase of the SDLC involves gathering requirements from users?

  • A) Systems Planning
  • B) Systems Analysis
  • C) Systems Design
  • D) Systems Implementation

Correct Answer: B) Systems Analysis

The Systems Analysis phase focuses on understanding the current system and determining the requirements for the new system through interviews, observations, and JAD sessions.

Part 3: Alternative Methodologies

Beyond the SDLC

Limitations of the SDLC

The SDLC is great for structured, stable projects (like payroll), but has drawbacks:

  • Rigid: Hard to go back once a phase is done ("Waterfall").
  • Slow: Takes a long time to see a working product.
  • Requirements Issues: Users often don't know what they want until they see it.

Prototyping

Uses a trial-and-error approach for discovering how a system should operate.

graph LR A[Collect Reqs] --> B[Build Prototype]; B --> C[User Review]; C -->|Changes Needed| A; C -->|Approved| D[Final System]; style A fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style B fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style C fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5 style D fill:#eef2ff,stroke:#4338ca

Allows for rapid feedback and refinement of requirements.

Agile Methodologies

Definition

An evolutionary development approach that focuses on creating small, client-approved parts of the system as the project progresses.

  • Iterative: Developed in short "sprints" (2-4 weeks).
  • Adaptive: Welcomes changing requirements.
  • Collaborative: Self-managing teams working closely with customers.

Examples: Scrum, eXtreme Programming (XP).

graph TD subgraph Waterfall A[Plan] --> B[Analyze] --> C[Design] --> D[Build] end subgraph Agile E[Sprint 1] --> F[Sprint 2] --> G[Sprint 3] end style A fill:#e0f2fe style B fill:#e0f2fe style C fill:#e0f2fe style D fill:#e0f2fe style E fill:#dbeafe style F fill:#dbeafe style G fill:#dbeafe

Agile vs. Waterfall

Waterfall (SDLC):
Linear, predictable. Best when requirements are well understood and stable (e.g., Engineering).

Agile:
Fluid, unpredictable. Best for dynamic projects where requirements change (e.g., Consumer Apps, Startups).

Agile prioritizes working software over comprehensive documentation.

DevOps

Historically, "Development" (creating systems) and "Operations" (running systems) were separate silos. This caused friction.

DevOps

A culture and practice that integrates software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops). It aims to shorten the systems development life cycle and provide continuous delivery with high software quality.

Knowledge Check

Which methodology utilizes short iterations or "sprints" to deliver working software frequently?

  • A) SDLC
  • B) Waterfall
  • C) Agile
  • D) Prototyping

Correct Answer: C) Agile

Agile methodologies like Scrum use iterative cycles called sprints to deliver small, working pieces of the system, allowing for rapid feedback and adaptation.

Part 4: External Acquisition & Outsourcing

Buying Instead of Building

External Acquisition

Purchasing an existing system from an outside vendor (e.g., IBM, Oracle, SAP).

Reasons to Acquire:

  • Limited IS Staff: Too small or busy.
  • Limited Skill Set: Lack of specific expertise.
  • Performance Issues: Internal staff is overworked or underperforming.

It is similar to buying a car: You analyze needs, shop around, test drive, and buy.

Steps in External Acquisition

  1. Systems Planning and Selection: (Same as SDLC)
  2. Systems Analysis: (Same as SDLC)
  3. Development of a Request for Proposal (RFP): Asking vendors to bid.
  4. Proposal Evaluation: Comparing bids.
  5. Vendor Selection: Choosing the winner.

Request for Proposal (RFP)

Definition

A document used to tell vendors what your requirements are and to invite them to provide information about how they might be able to meet those requirements.

Includes: Summary of existing systems, performance requirements, reliability needs, evaluation criteria, and budget/timetable.

Proposal Evaluation

How do you choose the best vendor?

  • Demonstrations: Vendor presents the system ("Test Drive").
  • Benchmarking: Using standardized tests to compare performance (e.g., time to process 10,000 records).
  • Criteria: Cost, functionality, support, vendor reputation.

Software Licensing

When you buy software, you are buying a license to use it, not the software itself.

Shrink-Wrap / Click-Wrap

Generic, off-the-shelf licenses. Activated when you open the box or click "I Accept".

Enterprise License

Volume licenses negotiated for large organizations. Often includes limitations of liability.

Outsourcing Systems Development

Turning over responsibility for some or all of an organization's IS development and operations to an outside firm (e.g., Accenture, Infosys).

Why Outsource?

  • Cost and Quality Concerns (Economies of scale).
  • Problems in IS Performance.
  • Focus on Core Competencies (Simplify/Downsize).
  • Financial Factors (Liquidate IT assets).

Managing Outsourcing

Outsourcing is not "fire and forget". It requires active management.

  • Strong CIO: To manage the legal and professional relationship.
  • Performance Measurements: Clear, realistic metrics for the vendor.
  • Multiple Levels of Interface: Operational, tactical, and strategic links between the firms.

Strategic Outsourcing

What to Outsource?
Strategic tasks (e.g., Apple's design) should stay in-house. Non-strategic tasks (e.g., manufacturing, payroll) are candidates for outsourcing.

When to Outsource?
Non-strategic tasks can be outsourced anytime. Strategic tasks might be outsourced temporarily if time-to-market is critical, but should eventually be brought in-house.

  • B) Request for Proposal (RFP)

Key Takeaways

  • Business Case: Essential for justifying investments using Faith, Fear, and Fact.
  • SDLC: A structured process (Planning, Analysis, Design, Implementation) for building systems.
  • Agile: An iterative alternative to SDLC for dynamic environments.
  • Acquisition: Buying (COTS) is often faster and cheaper than building (Custom).
  • Outsourcing: Moving functions to outside providers requires careful management.
