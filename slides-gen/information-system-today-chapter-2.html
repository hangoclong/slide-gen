Chapter Preview

This chapter explores how organizations use Information Systems (IS) to gain a strategic edge over rivals.

A firm has a competitive advantage when it can do something better, faster, more economically, or uniquely.

We will cover:

  • The role of IS at different organizational levels.
  • How IS enables innovative business models.
  • The continuous need for innovation to succeed with IS.

Startups and New Business Models

Advances in information technology have enabled entirely new, and often disruptive, business models.

These models leverage technology to create, deliver, and capture value in novel ways.

Just because a business model is enabled by technology, however, does not guarantee it will be successful.

Examples of Tech-Enabled Business Models

Platform

Enable others to co-create value. (e.g., Airbnb, Uber)

Disintermediation

Bypass traditional channels to connect directly with customers. (e.g., Warby Parker, Casper)

Subscription

Offer services for a recurring fee, removing transaction friction. (e.g., Netflix, Spotify)

On-Demand

Use mobile and location-based tech to provide immediate services. (e.g., Uber)

Knowledge Check

A company like Warby Parker, which sells eyeglasses directly to consumers online, is an example of which business model?

  • A) Platform
  • B) Subscription
  • C) On-Demand
  • D) Disintermediation

Correct Answer: D

Disintermediation means cutting out the middleman. Warby Parker bypasses traditional optical shops and sells directly to the customer, which is a classic example of this model.

Part 2: IS and Organizational Strategy

Aligning Systems with Goals

Organizational Decision-Making Levels

Every organization has different levels, each with unique responsibilities and information needs. Understanding this structure helps illustrate how different types of IS can provide value.

graph TD A[Executive Level] --> B[Managerial Level]; B --> C[Operational Level]; style A fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5,stroke-width:2px style B fill:#e0e7ff,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px style C fill:#eef2ff,stroke:#312e81,stroke-width:2px

1. The Operational Level

This is where the routine, day-to-day business processes and customer interactions occur.

  • Who: Foremen, Supervisors, front-line employees.
  • Decisions: Highly structured and recurring (e.g., reordering inventory, processing sales). These are structured decisions where procedures can be specified in advance.
  • IS Goal: To increase efficiency by automating repetitive tasks.
  • Example: An inventory management system that automatically places an order when stock is low.

2. The Managerial (Tactical) Level

Functional managers monitor and control operational activities and provide information to higher-ups.

  • Who: Midlevel managers (e.g., marketing, finance, HR managers).
  • Decisions: Moderately complex, with a time horizon of a few days to months. These are semistructured decisions that require judgment and expertise.
  • IS Goal: To increase effectiveness by helping managers make better decisions.
  • Example: A business intelligence dashboard showing KPIs to help a manager allocate an advertising budget.

3. The Executive (Strategic) Level

Managers focus on long-term strategic questions facing the entire organization.

  • Who: President, CEO, CIO, Vice Presidents, Board of Directors.
  • Decisions: Complex, non-routine, with long-term ramifications. These are unstructured decisions with no predefined procedures.
  • IS Goal: To improve strategy and planning with summaries of past data and future projections.
  • Example: An executive dashboard showing industry trends and competitor performance to decide whether to enter a new market.

Decision Levels at a Glance

E
Executive: Unstructured, long-term, strategic decisions. Focus on "Why?"
M
Managerial: Semistructured, tactical decisions. Focus on "How?"
O
Operational: Structured, day-to-day decisions. Focus on "What?"

Each level requires different information systems to support its unique decision-making needs.

Knowledge Check

A marketing manager deciding on the advertising budget for the next quarter is operating at which level?

  • A) Operational Level
  • B) Managerial Level
  • C) Executive Level
  • D) Strategic Level

Correct Answer: B

This is a semistructured, tactical decision focused on effectively deploying resources within a specific business function (marketing) to achieve strategic objectives. This is characteristic of the managerial or tactical level.

How IS Provides Value

Information systems can add value to a business in three progressive ways. Each builds upon the last, offering greater benefits to the firm.

graph LR A[Automating] --> B[Learning]; B --> C[Strategizing]; style A fill:#eef2ff,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px style B fill:#e0e7ff,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px style C fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px

Let's explore each of these stages.

1. IS for Automating: Doing Things Faster

The first and most basic use of IS is to automate tasks to make them faster, cheaper, and more accurate.

Automating Mentality

Focuses on using technology to increase efficiency. For example, automating a manual loan application process to reduce processing time and errors.

This is often the focus of IS at the operational level, where quick returns on investment can be realized by optimizing repetitive activities.

2. IS for Learning: Doing Things Better

This goes beyond simple automation. It involves using the data generated by automated processes to improve the processes themselves.

Learning Mentality

Recognizes that IS can be a vehicle for organizational learning—the ability to use past behavior and data to improve business processes and increase effectiveness.

For example, analyzing loan application data to identify trends, plan staffing, and refine the criteria for loan approval.

The Danger of Automating a Bad Process

Automating without learning can be dangerous. If the underlying business process is flawed, technology will only magnify the problem.

  • Manual Flawed Process: A loan officer manually approves 2 "bad" loans per week.
  • Automated Flawed Process: The same faulty logic is automated, and the system now helps the officer approve 6 "bad" loans per week.

Without a learning component to analyze outcomes and identify the flawed criteria, the organization is simply making mistakes faster.

3. IS for Strategy: Doing Things Smarter

The highest level of value comes from using IS to support the organization's core strategy and gain a competitive advantage.

Strategic Mentality

Goes beyond automating and learning to find ways to use IS to achieve the organization's chosen strategy—by innovating, streamlining operations, or better understanding customers.

Today, if a proposed IS doesn't deliver clear strategic value, it's unlikely to be funded. This is known as maximizing business/IT alignment.

Knowledge Check

An organization that analyzes sales data to identify its most profitable customers and then tailors marketing campaigns to them is primarily using IS for:

  • A) Automating
  • B) Organizational Learning
  • C) Supporting Strategy
  • D) Both B and C

Correct Answer: D

The company is using past data to improve its processes (Learning), and it is doing so to achieve a specific goal of targeting profitable customers, which supports its overall business strategy (Strategy). This demonstrates both learning and strategic use of IS.

Part 3: Frameworks for Competitive Advantage

Identifying How and Where to Compete

Where to Compete: Porter's Five Forces

To understand where to focus resources, organizations analyze the competitive forces within their industry. Porter's Five Forces model is a classic framework for this analysis.

  1. Rivalry among existing competitors
  2. Threat of new entrants
  3. Bargaining power of customers
  4. Bargaining power of suppliers
  5. Threat of substitute products/services

The Five Forces Model

This model helps a company understand the structure of its industry and its profit potential.

For example, the internet has increased the bargaining power of customers by giving them wider choices and lower switching costs, while also reducing barriers to entry for new entrants.

By understanding these forces, a company can identify opportunities to use IS to counter their effects.

How to Compete: Choosing a Generic Strategy

Once a company understands its industry, it must choose a strategy to position itself. Porter identified several generic strategies:

Low-Cost Leadership

Offer the best prices in the industry (e.g., Walmart).

Differentiation

Provide better products or services than competitors (e.g., Porsche, Apple).

Best-Cost Provider

Offer products of reasonably good quality at competitive prices (e.g., Dell).

Generic Strategy Matrix

Strategies can also be focused on a narrow market segment (a niche) or applied broadly across the industry.

  • Focused Low-Cost: Targeting a specific niche with the lowest prices.
  • Focused Differentiation: Offering unique products to a specific niche.

The chosen strategy dictates where IS investments should be focused to create the most value.

How to Compete: Value Chain Analysis

This process helps identify opportunities where IS can be used to gain a competitive advantage.

Value Chain

The set of activities an organization performs to create, deliver, and support its products or services. Each step should add value.

Value chain analysis involves drawing the chain, determining the costs and drivers for each activity, and identifying which activities to optimize.

The Value Chain

Primary Activities

  • Inbound Logistics
  • Operations
  • Outbound Logistics
  • Marketing and Sales
  • Service

Support Activities

  • Firm Infrastructure, HR, Technology Development, Procurement

IS can be used to automate and optimize activities all along the value chain.

Knowledge Check

A company like Walmart, which uses massive-scale IS to optimize its supply chain and offer the lowest prices, is executing which generic strategy?

  • A) Differentiation
  • B) Low-Cost Leadership
  • C) Focused Differentiation
  • D) Best-Cost Provider

Correct Answer: B

Walmart's entire business model is built around operational efficiency to drive down costs and offer the lowest prices to a broad market, which is the definition of a low-cost leadership strategy.

Part 4: Business Models in the Digital World

Creating and Capturing Value

What is a Business Model?

A business model is a summary of a business's strategic direction that outlines how objectives will be achieved.

It specifies the value proposition and how the company will create, deliver, and capture value.

It answers key questions:

  • What does the company do?
  • How does it uniquely do it?
  • How does it get paid?
  • What are the key resources, activities, and costs?

Revenue Models in the Digital World

A revenue model describes how the firm will earn revenue and generate profits. The internet has enabled several innovative models:

Affiliate Marketing

Paying businesses that refer customers to you (e.g., Amazon's Associates Program).

Advertising

Free services paid for by a third party (e.g., Google, Facebook).

Transaction Fees

Taking a commission for aiding in a transaction (e.g., PayPal, eBay, Airbnb).

Freemium

Basic services are free; premium features cost money (e.g., Dropbox, Spotify).

Focus on: The Freemium Model

The term combines "free" and "premium." It's a popular model for digital products where the marginal cost of an additional user is near zero.

How it Works

An organization gives away limited versions of a product for free to build a large customer base, then charges a premium for unrestricted versions.

The challenge is balancing the free offering: it must be compelling enough to attract users, but not so feature-rich that no one pays for the premium version.

Freemium in Action: Dropbox

  • Free Tier: Offers a small amount of cloud storage (e.g., 2GB) for free. This attracts a massive user base.
  • Premium Tier: Users who need more storage can subscribe to a paid plan with significantly more space and additional features.
  • Benefit: The large base of free users acts as a powerful marketing engine through word-of-mouth referrals.

This model is extremely popular for mobile apps, games (with in-app purchases), and software-as-a-service (SaaS) products.

Platform-Based Business Models

Traditionally, businesses operated like pipes, where products were produced and pushed to customers.

Increasing digital density has enabled platforms, which are digital ecosystems where some users create value and other users consume it.

Platform

A business model that coordinates other businesses and users to co-create value. The platform owner doesn't create the service, but facilitates the connection.

Examples: YouTube (content), eBay (products), Uber (services), Kickstarter (funding).

The Two-Sided Market & Network Effects

Platforms create a two-sided market, and their success depends on the network effect: the value of the platform increases as more users join.

graph TD A[Producers] --> C{Platform}; C --> B[Consumers]; B --> C; subgraph Network A B end style C fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5,stroke-width:2px

A platform is only valuable if it attracts both producers (e.g., Uber drivers, Airbnb hosts) and consumers (riders, guests).

Service-Based Business Models (XaaS)

This model, often called "X as a Service," involves selling the *outcome* or *use* of a product, rather than the product itself.

  • Traditional Model: GE sells a jet engine to an airline. The airline owns it and pays for maintenance.
  • Service Model: GE provides "thrust as a service." The airline pays for hours of usage, and GE guarantees uptime, using IoT sensors to predict and perform maintenance.

This transforms a customer's fixed costs (buying an asset) into variable costs (paying for usage) and aligns the incentives of the manufacturer and customer.

Knowledge Check

YouTube does not create its own videos, but provides a space for creators to upload content and for viewers to watch it. This is an example of a:

  • A) Pipe-based model
  • B) Freemium model
  • C) Platform-based model
  • D) Service-based model

Correct Answer: C

YouTube is a classic platform. It creates a digital ecosystem that connects two distinct groups of users—content creators (producers) and viewers (consumers)—to co-create value.

Part 5: The Innovation Imperative

Staying Ahead in the Digital World

Valuing Innovations

Innovation is key for gaining or sustaining a competitive advantage. It involves creating new products, processes, or services that return value to the organization.

Incremental Innovation

Enhancing or upgrading existing products, services, or processes. (e.g., a new iPhone model with a better camera).

Radical (Disruptive) Innovation

Uses a new technology to access new customers or provide greater benefits, eventually replacing existing products (e.g., digital photography replacing film).

Example of Radical Innovation

The rise of music streaming services like Spotify completely disrupted the music industry.

  • Displaced Technology: CDs, cassettes, and even MP3 players and music stores.
  • New Business Model: Shifted from owning music (product) to accessing a vast library of music (service) via a subscription model.

Radical innovations often create new markets and value networks, displacing established market leaders.

The Need for Constant Innovation

Gaining a competitive advantage through IS is difficult and often short-lived.

  • Innovation is Fleeting: Rivals can copy innovative systems, products, and processes. An advantage gained today may be gone tomorrow.
  • Innovation is Risky: Choosing the wrong technology can be a costly mistake (e.g., the Betamax vs. VHS format war).
  • Innovation is Difficult: The pace of technological change is so fast that it's nearly impossible to predict the "next big thing."

Harnessing External Ideas: Open Innovation

Given the difficulties of innovating internally, organizations are increasingly looking outside their own walls for new ideas.

Open Innovation

The process of integrating external stakeholders (customers, suppliers, universities, the public) into the innovation process.

Platforms like Lego Ideas (customer-submitted designs) and InnoCentive (scientific problem-solving marketplace) allow companies to harness the creativity of the crowd.

A Framework for Innovation: The Lean Startup

Pioneered by Eric Ries, this methodology provides a framework for navigating uncertainty and developing new products and business models.

Instead of writing a detailed business plan and then executing it, the lean startup approach focuses on a continuous cycle of experimentation and learning.

The core idea is to first search for a viable product and business model, and only execute once a fit between the product and the market has been found.

The Build-Measure-Learn Cycle

The lean startup methodology is powered by a feedback loop called the Build-Measure-Learn cycle.

graph TD A(Build) --> B(Measure); B --> C(Learn); C --> A; style A fill:#eef2ff,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px style B fill:#e0e7ff,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px style C fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px

This cycle enables validated learning—empirically testing assumptions through customer feedback to improve future iterations.

The Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

A key concept in the "Build" phase is the MVP.

Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

A version of a product with just enough features to be usable by early customers who can then provide feedback for future product development.

The goal of an MVP is not to be a perfect, feature-complete product. Its purpose is to start the learning process as quickly and cheaply as possible.

Visualizing the MVP

Instead of building a product piece by piece (which delivers no value until the end), the MVP approach focuses on delivering a functional, albeit basic, version at each stage.

You don't build a wheel, then an axle, then a chassis. You build a skateboard, then a scooter, then a bicycle, then a car.

Each iteration is a usable product that solves the core problem and allows you to learn from real users.

Pivoting: Changing Direction

If the Build-Measure-Learn cycle reveals that your initial hypotheses are wrong, it's time to pivot.

Pivot

A structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, strategy, or engine of growth.

Example: Instagram started as "Burbn," a complex app with check-ins and gaming. After realizing users only valued the photo-sharing feature, the founders pivoted, stripping the app down to its core. This pivot led to massive success.

Knowledge Check

In the lean startup methodology, what is the primary purpose of a Minimum Viable Product (MVP)?

  • A) To generate maximum revenue from early adopters.
  • B) To be the final, polished version of the product.
  • C) To begin the learning process with minimal effort and resources.
  • D) To secure a patent for the product idea.

Correct Answer: C

The goal of an MVP is not to be a perfect product, but to be a tool for learning. It's the fastest way to get through the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop with the least amount of effort, allowing you to test your core assumptions with real users.

Organizational Requirements for Innovation

For established organizations to innovate like a startup, they need the right culture and structure.

  • Process Requirements: Must allow for cross-functional teams and startup-like budgeting. Incentives should be at the team level, not individual.
  • Resource Requirements: Must have people with the skills, time, and resources to build MVPs quickly.
  • Risk Tolerance Requirements: Must have an appropriate tolerance for risk, uncertainty, and the willingness to "fail fast."

Funding Innovation: Crowdfunding

A novel way for startups to get funding has been through crowdfunding platforms.

Crowdfunding

The securing of business financing from individuals in the marketplace—the "crowd"—to fund an initiative, typically in exchange for the product or equity.

Platforms like Kickstarter and Indiegogo allow inventors and startups to pitch ideas directly to potential customers and raise capital without traditional investors.

Session Recap: Key Takeaways

  • IS can be used for automating (faster), learning (better), and strategy (smarter).
  • Frameworks like Porter's Five Forces and Value Chain Analysis help identify where and how to compete.
  • The digital world has enabled new business models like platforms, freemium, and XaaS.
  • Continuous innovation is essential for survival, but it is often risky and difficult.
  • The Lean Startup methodology (Build-Measure-Learn, MVP, Pivot) provides a framework for navigating the uncertainty of innovation.
