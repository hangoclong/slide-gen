Preview: The Power of Connection

Enterprise-wide information systems are key to building and strengthening organizational partnerships.

Integration is Key

Systems must span the entire organization to tie everything together. This includes Supply Chain Management (SCM) for upstream B2B partners and Customer Relationship Management (CRM) for downstream customers.

In today's competitive digital world, understanding how to manage these relationships is critical for success.

Case Study: Walmart's Supply Chain

Walmart is the world's largest retailer, known for its relentless pursuit of low costs. Its success is largely attributed to its highly efficient supply chain.

  • Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI): Manufacturers monitor inventory levels in Walmart's warehouses.
  • Cross-Docking: Direct transfers from inbound to outbound trucks without extra storage.
  • Data Warehouse: Records every purchase globally to optimize stocking and clustering of items.

Visualizing Cross Docking

This "just-in-time" approach keeps products moving and reduces warehousing expenses.

Supply Chain Management (SCM)

Coordinating with suppliers to improve efficiency and effectiveness.

Why Collaborate?

When deliveries are accurate and timely, companies can convert raw materials into finished products more efficiently. This reduces inventory costs and speeds up time-to-market.

Supply Network vs. Chain: The flow of materials is complex, involving multiple suppliers and customers, forming a network rather than a linear chain.

Real-world supply chains are complex networks of interdependencies.

Business-to-Business (B2B) E-Commerce

90% of all EC is B2B. It involves exchanging proprietary information like orders and invoices.

  • Electronic Data Interchange (EDI): Computer-to-computer communication of standard business documents. Replaces paper.
  • Extranets: Using the internet to exchange data securely with partners.
  • Portals: Access points for business partners to secured, proprietary information.

Portals: Connecting Partners

Supplier Portals

Owned by the Buyer.

Connects a single buyer with multiple suppliers.

Used for: Sourcing, Procurement, Managing terms.

Example: HP Supplier Portal.

Customer Portals

Owned by the Supplier.

Connects a single supplier with multiple business customers.

Used for: Sales, Customer Service, Order Tracking.

Example: MyBoeingFleet.

B2B Marketplaces

Operated by third-party vendors. They bring together many buyers and many sellers.

  • Vertical Markets: Focused on specific industries (e.g., paper, steel, textiles).
  • General Markets: Alibaba.com connects buyers and suppliers from every industry globally.
  • Revenue Model: Transaction commissions, usage fees, or advertising.

Managing Complex Supply Networks: Apple

The iPhone says "Designed in California, Assembled in China." This hides a massive global complexity.

Global Sourcing

  • Flash Memory/CPU: Samsung (Korea)
  • Display: LG (Korea)
  • Chips: Infineon (Germany), Broadcom (USA)
  • Assembly: Foxconn (China)

Coordinating this requires managing risks like shortages, natural disasters, and political issues.

Just-in-Time (JIT) Production

Based on the notion that keeping inventory is costly (storage, capital, obsolescence).

  • Concept: Parts arrive just when needed for production.
  • Example: Dell keeps only about 2 hours of inventory.
  • Requirement: Tight cooperation and reliable logistics are essential. Disruptions can stop the line immediately.

Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI)

A business model where the suppliers manage the manufacturer's (or retailer's) inventory levels.

Manufacturer Benefits

Reduced inventory costs.

Minimized stockouts.

Offloaded management responsibility.

Supplier Benefits

Access to real-time sales data.

Better production forecasting.

Lock-in of the customer relationship.

The Bullwhip Effect

Small fluctuations in downstream demand can cause massive fluctuations in upstream supply chain needs.

graph LR C[Customer Demand: +/- 5%] --> R[Retailer Order: +/- 10%] R --> D[Distributor Order: +/- 20%] D --> M[Manufacturer Prod: +/- 40%] style C fill:#d1fae5,stroke:#065f46 style M fill:#fee2e2,stroke:#991b1b

Cause: Forecast errors and safety buffers multiply at each stage.

Solution: Integrated information systems (SCM) to share real-time demand data across the chain.

SCM and Corporate Social Responsibility

Transparency in the supply chain is critical for CSR.

  • Product Recalls: Tracing products back to the source (e.g., a specific batch of contaminated lettuce) saves money and lives.
  • Sustainable Practices: Ethical treatment of workers and environmental impact. Companies like Nike and HP must monitor their suppliers to maintain their own reputation.

SCM systems consist of modules focusing on Planning and Execution.

Supply Chain Planning (SCP)

Developing resource plans to support production.

  1. Demand Planning: Forecasting demand based on history and other factors (promotions, seasons).
  2. Distribution Planning: Planning the movement of goods to distributors and warehouses.
  3. Production Scheduling: Coordinating manufacturing activities and resources.
  4. Inventory Planning: Determining optimal safety stock levels.

Supply Chain Execution (SCE)

Putting the plan into motion. It manages three key flows:

Product Flow

Movement of goods from supplier to consumer.

Also includes Reverse Logistics (Returns/Recycling).

Information Flow

Order processing and delivery status updates.

removal of paper documents.

Financial Flow

Payment schedules, consignment, and ownership data.

Linkages to electronic banking.

SCM Technology Enablers

  • XML (Extensible Markup Language): A standard for exchanging structured data over the web. It allows different systems to "talk" to each other (e.g., sending an invoice from System A to System B).
  • RFID (Radio Frequency Identification): Identifying and tracking items using radio waves. Replaces bar codes for faster, automated tracking of inventory.

Knowledge Check

Which phenomenon refers to small fluctuations in demand causing massive fluctuations in supply chain forecasts?

  • A) The Butterfly Effect
  • B) The Bullwhip Effect
  • C) The Supply Chain Ripple
  • D) The Inventory Paradox

Correct Answer: B

The Bullwhip Effect occurs when forecast errors and safety stocks multiply as they move upstream in the supply chain, causing inefficiency and waste.

Customer Relationship Management (CRM)

Competition is just a click away. Companies must attract and retain customers.

CRM Strategy

A corporate-level strategy to create and maintain lasting relationships by concentrating on downstream information flows.

Goals: Attract potential customers, create loyalty, and portray a positive image.

Maximizing Customer Value

CRM helps companies to:

It costs 50-100 times more to win back a lost customer than to keep a current one.

Architecture of a CRM System

A comprehensive CRM system has three primary components:

  1. Operational CRM: Systems for automating the fundamental business processes (marketing, sales, support).
  2. Analytical CRM: Analyzing customer behavior and perceptions to provide business intelligence.
  3. Collaborative CRM: Providing effective communication with the customer from the entire organization.

Operational CRM: Sales Force Automation (SFA)

Supports day-to-day sales activities.

  • Order Processing: Tracking orders and status.
  • Account Management: Managing contacts and opportunities.
  • Benefits: Reduces paperwork, improves sales forecasting, and gives managers a clear view of the "sales pipeline."

Operational CRM: Customer Service & Support

Automates service requests, complaints, and returns.

Customer Engagement Center (CEC)

Uses multiple channels (web, Facebook, phone, face-to-face) to support customers.

Goal: First-Call Resolution. Solving the customer's issue during the first contact saves costs and increases satisfaction.

Operational CRM: Enterprise Marketing Management

Improving the management of promotional campaigns.

  • Integrates campaigns across channels (email, social media, direct mail).
  • Ensures the right message goes to the right person at the right time.
  • Helps analyze campaign effectiveness (Conversion Rates).

Analytical CRM

Analyzing customer behavior to identify new opportunities.

  • Cross-Selling: Selling related products (e.g., a phone case with a phone).
  • Up-Selling: Selling a more expensive version (e.g., a premium subscription).
  • Customer Segmentation: Grouping customers to customize marketing messages.

Uses Data Mining and Predictive Modeling.

Social CRM

Engaging with customers via social media channels.

Customers share experiences on social media. Companies must monitor and participate in these conversations.

Social Media Monitoring: Identifying and assessing the volume and sentiment of what is being said about a company.

Example: Dell's Social Media Listening Command Center.

Collaborative CRM

Facilitates information sharing across the organization to serve the customer.

  • Integration: Ensures that Sales, Support, and Marketing all have the same view of the customer.
  • Reduced Handoffs: Customers don't have to repeat their story to different departments.
  • Customer Focus: Communication is tailored to the customer's history and preferences.

Ethical Concerns in CRM

CRM allows for highly personalized targeting, but it can cross the line.

The Creepiness Factor

When does personalization become invasion of privacy? relying too much on statistical profiles may categorize customers in ways they find offensive.

Companies must balance personalization with privacy concerns.

Industry Analysis: Manufacturing

Manufacturing is undergoing a digital transformation.

  • CAD (Computer-Aided Design): Speeds up product design.
  • 3D Printing (Additive Manufacturing): Allows for rapid prototyping and creating complex parts on demand. It reduces the need for large inventories of spare parts.
  • Implication: Potentially moving manufacturing back to local markets (reducing shipping costs).

Coming Attractions: Augmented Reality (AR) in SCM

AR overlays digital information onto the physical world.

Picking

AR headsets guide warehouse workers to the exact location of an item and show the best path, reducing errors.

Loading

AR can show how to efficiently stack boxes in a truck to maximize space.

Key Takeaways

  • SCM optimizes the flow of products, information, and funds from supplier to customer.
  • CRM manages the relationship with the customer to drive loyalty and revenue.
  • Integration of SCM, CRM, and ERP is essential for a seamless flow of information.
  • Collaboration via Portals and Marketplaces improves efficiency.
  • Technology like AI, RFID, and Blockchain is transforming these fields.
