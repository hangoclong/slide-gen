Preview: Social Media in Business

You use social media daily—Instagram, Facebook, Wikipedia. But why study it in a business context?

Social media introduces unprecedented ways to:

  • Connect with friends and colleagues.
  • Share knowledge instantly.
  • Collaborate with global teams 5,000 miles away.

Companies cannot afford to miss this trend. It is a critical skill for the modern workforce, and ignoring it can lead to losing talent and market relevance.

Managing in the Digital World: The Facebook Phenomenon

Interactions between customers and brands used to be one-way (TV ads, billboards). Today, it is dynamic and interactive.

  • Massive Scale: Billions of daily users.
  • Deep Connection: "Like" buttons and comments allow businesses to track loyalty and sentiment instantly.
  • Market Value: Social media giants have become some of the most valuable companies on earth.

Through platforms like Facebook, companies can interact with customers like never before, predicting future demands through collective tastes.

The Need for Communication and Collaboration

Organizations rely on effective communication and collaboration to achieve common goals.

Why is it essential?

Globalization has enabled companies to source materials and talent globally. Business processes now span departments, organizations, and national borders. Effective communication conveys specifications, coordinates schedules, and solves problems.

Without it, "silos" form, inefficiencies rise, and companies fail to compete in the global market.

Managing the Virtual Organization

To be competitive, organizations must bring together the right people with the right skills, regardless of location.

Virtual Teams

Teams composed of members from different geographic areas, assembled as needed to collaborate on a certain project.

  • Fluid Membership: Teams form and disband as needed.
  • 24/7 Operations: "Follow the sun" workflow (e.g., developers in India, managers in the US, testers in Europe).
  • Amplified by Pandemic: COVID-19 forced the world to adopt remote work, accelerating the virtual team trend.

Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Communication

Virtual teams need flexible tools to communicate across time zones.

Synchronous

At the same time.

Examples: Phone calls, Zoom meetings, Instant Messaging (IM), Face-to-face meetings.

Best for: Urgent issues, complex brainstorming.

Asynchronous

Not coordinated in time.

Examples: Email, Discussion Boards, Wikis, Shared Documents.

Best for: Global teams, non-urgent tasks, documentation.

Modern social media tools largely fill the "Different Time" and "Different Place" category.

Collaboration Software (Groupware)

Collaboration software enables people to work together more effectively.

  • Evolution: From Lotus Notes (1989) to modern cloud suites like Microsoft Teams and Google Workspace.
  • Key Dimensions:
    • Support for synchronous vs. asynchronous work.
    • Support for face-to-face vs. distributed groups.

Tools include electronic calendars, knowledge management systems, and online document systems.

Discussion Forums

One of the oldest and most widely used tools for asynchronous group communication.

  • Threaded Discussions: Allows conversations to be organized by topic and keeps history intact.
  • Moderation: Can be monitored to ensure relevance and accuracy.
  • Knowledge Base: Over time, a forum becomes a searchable repository of solutions (e.g., Stack Overflow, internal company support forums).

Modern tools like Slack and Microsoft Teams have evolved forums into "Channels," combining the speed of chat with the structure of threads.

Electronic Meeting Systems (EMS)

Meetings can be inefficient. EMS provides tools to help groups solve problems and make decisions.

1
Idea Generation: Participants type ideas anonymously to avoid "groupthink" or fear of criticism.
2
Idea Evaluation: The group categorizes and consolidates ideas.
3
Voting: Participants rank or vote on ideas to reach a consensus quickly.

Videoconferencing

From the 1960s "picturephone" to daily Zoom calls, video has become mainstream.

Desktop Videoconferencing

Simple, low-cost. Requires only a webcam and software (Skype, Teams, Zoom). Ideal for individuals and remote workers.

Dedicated Systems

High-end, realistic experience for conference rooms. Can cost thousands of dollars but offers high-fidelity audio and video for critical meetings.

Intranets and Employee Portals

Intranet

A private network using web technologies, used to facilitate the secured transmission of proprietary information within an organization.

  • Real-Time Access: Unlike paper, intranets provide up-to-the-minute corporate news and data.
  • Enterprise Search: Allows employees to find documents and data scattered across the organization.
  • Employee Portals: Self-service for HR benefits, travel management, and training. Reduces administrative costs significantly.

Knowledge Check

Which of the following best describes "asynchronous" communication?

  • A) Communication that occurs at the same time, like a phone call.
  • B) Communication that is not coordinated in time, like email.
  • C) Communication that requires a video connection.
  • D) Communication that only happens within an intranet.

Correct Answer: B

Asynchronous communication happens when participants do not need to be present at the same time. Examples include email, discussion forums, and shared documents, allowing flexibility for virtual teams in different time zones.

The Evolving Web: Web 1.0 vs. Web 2.0

The internet has shifted from a one-way medium to a collaborative platform.

Web 1.0 (The Passive Web)

Me focused.

Read-only content.

Companies create content; users consume it.

Example: Encyclopedia Britannica Online.

Web 2.0 (The Social Web)

Me and You.

Read/Write content.

Users create and share content (User-Generated Content).

Example: Wikipedia, YouTube.

Core Characteristics of Web 2.0

graph TD W2[Web 2.0] W2 --> I[Interactive] W2 --> U[User-Centered] W2 --> S[Social] W2 --> N[Network Effect] style W2 fill:#c7d2fe,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px style I fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style U fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style S fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9 style N fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9
  • Interactive: Shift from transaction to interaction.
  • User-Centered: Users rule, not techies.
  • Social: Connects ideas and people.
  • Network Effect: The value of the network increases with the number of users (e.g., Facebook is valuable because everyone is on it).

Web 2.0 applications shift the user's role from a passive consumer of content to its creator.

Mashups

A unique Web 2.0 capability is the ability to mix data from different sources.

Mashup

A new application (or website) that uses data from one or more service providers. It creates new value by combining existing services.

  • Powered by APIs: Application Programming Interfaces allow systems to talk to each other.
  • Example: A real estate site that combines Google Maps (location), Flickr (photos), and local crime data to give a complete view of a property.

The Evolving Workspace

The rise of Web 2.0 has coincided with the entry of Millennials (Generation Y) into the workforce.

  • New Expectations: They expect a "portfolio career" rather than a job for life.
  • Born Digital: They are accustomed to social tools and connectivity.
  • Culture Clash: If companies ban social media, these workers may leave or find workarounds.

To attract top talent, companies must embrace digital trends and open culture.

Future Web Capabilities: The Semantic Web

If Web 2.0 is about people connecting, the Semantic Web is about machines understanding.

The Semantic Web

A set of design principles that allow computers to better index and understand the meaning of web content, not just keywords.

  • Context Awareness: Searching for "Jaguar" and the computer knowing if you mean the car or the animal based on context.
  • Web 3.0: Often associated with mobility, "contextual web," and AI integration.

Knowledge Check

What is a "Mashup" in the context of Web 2.0?

  • A) A type of social network for music lovers.
  • B) An application that combines data from one or more service providers to create new value.
  • C) A conflict between two users on a wiki.
  • D) A security breach involving mixed data types.

Correct Answer: B

A mashup uses Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) to pull data from different sources (like maps, weather, and traffic) and combines them into a single, new application.

Enhancing Communication: Blogs

Blogging: Keeping an online text diary.

  • For Individuals: "Amateurization of journalism." Brings breaking news fast but can lack verification and objectivity.
  • For Business:
    • Connect with employees and customers.
    • Voice ideas and obtain feedback (e.g., Google's official blog).
    • Create a more "human" and approachable corporate image.

Enhancing Communication: Microblogging

Short status updates (e.g., Twitter/X, Weibo).

Key Features

Tweets: Short messages (up to 280 chars).
Hashtags (#): Tagging topics to make them searchable and trackable.
Trending: Topics that are currently popular due to user volume.

Companies use it for breaking news, customer support, and brand loyalty (e.g., Coca-Cola responding to followers).

Enhancing Communication: Instant Messaging (IM)

Real-time written conversations.

  • Enterprise Use: Tools like Slack or Microsoft Teams allow creating different channels for different projects, reducing email clutter.
  • Customer Service: Using WhatsApp or Web Chat for immediate support.
  • Chatbots: Automated agents that handle routine queries before routing to a human.

Enhancing Cooperation: Media Sharing

Sharing user-generated content (pictures, videos, audio) creates win-win situations.

Video/Image Sharing

Flickr, Instagram, YouTube. Companies use these to share product demos, culture videos, or viral campaigns.

Webcasting / Podcasting

Distribution of digital media for on-demand playback. Used for training, shareholder meetings, and education (iTunes U).

Enhancing Cooperation: Tagging & Metadata

User-generated content is vast. How do we organize it?

Metadata

Data about data (e.g., who took a photo, when, where). Tagging is manually adding metadata (keywords) to content.

  • Tag Clouds: Visualizations where the size of a word represents its frequency.
  • Geotagging: Adding geospatial metadata (latitude/longitude). Useful for mapping but raises privacy concerns (e.g., John McAfee's arrest due to metadata).

Enhancing Cooperation: Social Bookmarking

Allowing users to share interesting content and create categorization systems.

  • Folksonomies: Categorization systems created by users (socially), not experts.
  • Examples: Reddit, Pinterest, StumbleUpon.
  • Business Use: Mapping "islands" of knowledge within an organization. Identifying what employees find valuable.

Knowledge Check

What is "metadata"?

  • A) Data that is encrypted for security.
  • B) Data about data, such as the location or date of a photo.
  • C) A type of computer virus.
  • D) The physical storage medium for big data.

Correct Answer: B

Metadata describes other data. It provides context, such as who created a file, when it was created, or where a photo was taken, making content easier to categorize and find.

Enhancing Collaboration: Cloud-Based Tools

Cloud computing allows for easy access and transfer of documents.

  • Synchronized Storage: Dropbox, OneDrive, Google Drive. Latest version always available on all devices.
  • Productivity Suites: Google Workspace, Office 365. Multiple users can edit the same document simultaneously in real-time.
  • Benefit: No need to email attachments back and forth (version control).

Cloud-Based Collaboration: Benefits vs. Risks

Benefits

Reduced costs.

Easy to use & deploy.

Access from anywhere.

No software upgrades needed.

Risks

Loss of control over data.

Security/Compliance issues.

Tools may change without notice.

Requires internet connection.

Content Management Systems (CMS)

Systems that allow users to publish, edit, version track, and retrieve digital content.

  • Websites: WordPress, Joomla, Drupal. Allows non-technical users to update websites.
  • Learning: Learning Management Systems (LMS) like Blackboard or Moodle for education.
  • Digital Assets: Managing documents and corporate files (e.g., SharePoint).

Collective Intelligence

Wisdom of Crowds

The concept that distributed groups of people with diverse expertise will outperform individual experts.

  • Open Source Software: Firefox, Linux. Created and maintained by thousands of volunteers.
  • Peer Production: Creation of goods/services by self-organizing communities.

Wikis

Websites allowing people to create, edit, or delete content collaboratively.

  • History Tracking: Keeps all prior versions, allowing reverts of changes.
  • Wikipedia: The most famous example. High quality due to the sheer number of contributors monitoring edits.
  • Corporate Use: Internal knowledge repositories, project documentation.

Wikis rely on the community to minimize vandalism and ensure accuracy.

Crowdsourcing

Using everyday people as a cheap labor force.

  • Micro-Tasking: Amazon Mechanical Turk. Users solve small tasks (HITs) that computers can't do easily (e.g., identifying objects in photos).
  • Open Innovation: Asking the crowd for R&D ideas.
  • Citizen Science: Ordinary people contributing data to scientific projects.

Enhancing Connection: Social Networking

Social online communities where individuals meet, communicate, and collaborate.

  • General: Facebook. 2.6+ billion users. Leverages the network effect.
  • Professional: LinkedIn. For jobs, business contacts, and industry news.
  • Enterprise: Yammer, Slack. Private social networks for internal company use.

Viral Marketing

Using the network effect to increase brand awareness.

Viral Marketing

Marketing techniques driven by word-of-mouth, spreading like a virus through social networks.

Critical Factors (Thomas Baekdal):

  1. Do something unexpected.
  2. Make people feel something.
  3. Make sequels.
  4. Allow sharing and easy distribution.

Example: Hotmail's "Get your free email" footer strategy.

Knowledge Check

Which of the following is an example of "Crowdsourcing"?

  • A) Using cloud storage to back up files.
  • B) Using Amazon Mechanical Turk to get people to tag thousands of images.
  • C) Sending an email to a colleague.
  • D) Installing antivirus software.

Correct Answer: B

Crowdsourcing involves outsourcing tasks to a distributed group of people (the crowd). Mechanical Turk is a prime example where small "Human Intelligence Tasks" are distributed to workers globally.

Managing Enterprise Social Media

Success in the consumer world does not guarantee success in the corporate world. Management is key.

Culture

Requires a culture of openness, trust, and sharing. Top-down hierarchies often resist social tools.

Context

Must solve a specific business need. Don't just use a wiki because it's "cool."

Critical Mass

Needs enough users to create value (Network Effect). Hard for small companies.

Organizational Pitfalls

  • Generation Gap: Millennials embrace these tools; Baby Boomers may resist or not understand them.
  • Technological Inertia: People are resistant to change unless they see immediate benefits.
  • Security: increased sharing means increased risk of data leaks or IP theft.

Dangers: Online Product Reviews

Reviews are critical for purchasing decisions, but they are easily manipulated.

  • Fake Reviews: Companies hire people to post positive reviews for themselves or negative ones for competitors.
  • Unethical: Undermines trust in the review system.
  • Detection: Sites like Fakespot analyze language patterns to identify fake reviews.

Dangers: Microblogging Blunders

Posting without thinking can be disastrous.

Example: #myNYPD

The NYPD encouraged people to tweet photos with officers to boost their image. Instead, critics flooded the hashtag with photos of police brutality. The campaign backfired completely.

For individuals, ranting about a boss or posting inappropriate content can lead to being fired.

When Bad Vibes Go Viral

The internet never forgets, and negative news travels fast.

  • United Breaks Guitars: A musician's $3,500 guitar was broken by baggage handlers. United refused to pay. He made a music video that got 15 million views. United's stock dropped.
  • Domino's Pizza: Employees posted a prank video contaminating food. It went viral. Domino's had to respond with a CEO video apology to restore trust.

Social Media Crisis Management

How to handle a PR disaster in the digital age (Tips from Richard Levick):

  1. Identify a Crisis Team: PR, execs, lawyers.
  2. Identify Your Nightmare: Know what could go wrong.
  3. Monitor Social Media: Listen to what is being said (Social Listening).
  4. Act Fast: The first 24 hours are critical. A slow response looks like indifference.

Societal Impacts of Social Media

Positives

Democratized content creation.

Lowered barriers to entry for politics (e.g., small donor funding).

Wider access to information.

Negatives

Filter Bubbles / Echo Chambers.

Fake News & Clickbait.

Algorithms replacing editorial judgment.

The Viral Campaign Strategy

Scenario: You are the social media team for a new eco-friendly water bottle brand. You need to create a viral marketing campaign concept.

Task: Use ChatGPT or Gemini to brainstorm.

Sample Prompts:

  • "Generate 3 viral marketing campaign ideas for a sustainable water bottle brand that targets Gen Z. Focus on humor and 'doing something unexpected'."
  • "What are some trending hashtags related to sustainability and hydration right now?"
  • "Draft a script for a 30-second TikTok video that could go viral for this product."

Output: Present your best campaign idea to the class. Why will it go viral? What are the risks?

Key Takeaways

  • Social media enhances organizational communication, collaboration, and connection.
  • Virtual teams rely on synchronous and asynchronous tools to work globally.
  • Web 2.0 shifted the web from static content to user-generated, social interaction.
  • Enterprise tools (Wikis, Cloud, Intranets) harness collective intelligence.
  • Managing social media requires understanding culture, monitoring for crises, and acting fast when things go wrong.
