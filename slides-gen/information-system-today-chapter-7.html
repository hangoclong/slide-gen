Preview: Integrating the Enterprise

To succeed in today's competitive world, companies need systems that span the entire organization.

Enterprise Systems

Information systems that integrate business activities across departmental boundaries. They increase coordination, streamline operations, and improve responsiveness to customers.

This chapter focuses on internally focused systems. Chapter 8 will cover systems that span multiple organizations (externally focused).

Case Study: Amazon

Amazon has transformed from an online bookseller to an "everything store" by mastering enterprise-wide information systems.

  • Optimization: Amazon uses IS to optimize processes from acquiring goods to delivering packages (sometimes by drone!).
  • Integration: It manages fulfillment for thousands of small businesses, turning warehousing into a revenue stream.
  • Innovation: AWS (Amazon Web Services) allows other companies to run their enterprise systems in the cloud.

Core Business Processes

Traditionally, companies are organized into functional areas (Marketing, Accounting, Manufacturing). However, business processes cross these boundaries.

The Customer's View

Customers don't care about departments; they care about results. Business processes should be viewed as a series of value-added activities from the customer's perspective.

We will examine three core revenue-generating processes.

1. Order-to-Cash

The process of selling goods or services and collecting revenue.

Key Steps:

  1. Create Customer Record
  2. Check Credit
  3. Create Order
  4. Allocate Stock
  5. Pick, Pack, Ship
  6. Invoice & Collect Payment

Inefficiencies here lead to lost sales and unhappy customers.

2. Procure-to-Pay

The process of ordering goods or services and paying for them.

Key Steps:

  1. Negotiate Price & Terms
  2. Issue Purchase Order
  3. Receive Goods
  4. Receive Invoice
  5. Settle Payment

Effective procurement reduces costs and builds strong supplier relationships.

3. Make-to-Stock vs. Make-to-Order

The processes associated with producing goods.

Make-to-Stock (Push)

Goods are produced based on forecasts and stocked in a warehouse.

Example: Televisions, Home Appliances.

Make-to-Order (Pull)

Manufacturing starts only after an order is received.

Example: Dell Computers, Commercial Aircraft.

Supply Chains

Core processes enable the creation of supply chains.

Analogy: A River

Raw materials start at the source and move downstream toward the end customer. At each step, value is added.

  • Upstream: Flow from suppliers to the organization.
  • Downstream: Flow from the organization to customers.

The Value Chain Framework

Information systems improve effectiveness (value) and efficiency (cost) of the value chain.

Core Activities: Logistics & Operations

Inbound Logistics

Receiving and stocking raw materials, parts, and products. Crucial for the procure-to-pay process.

Operations & Manufacturing

Transforming inputs into outputs. Order processing (Amazon) or assembly (Dell).

Outbound Logistics

Distribution of end products. Picking, packing, and shipping to the customer.

Core Activities: Marketing & Service

  • Marketing & Sales: Presales activities. Creating marketing literature, pricing, and communicating with customers (e.g., website, social media).
  • Customer Service: Post-sales activities. Answering questions, tracking orders, and handling returns. Self-service portals (Amazon) reduce costs.

Support Activities

Activities that enable the core activities to take place.

  • Administration: Day-to-day orchestration of operations.
  • Infrastructure: Buildings, machinery, and IS components (networks, databases).
  • Human Resources: Hiring, training, payroll, and benefits.
  • Technology Development: Designing and developing applications to support the business.
  • Procurement: Purchasing goods and services required as inputs.

Knowledge Check

Which of the following is a Core Activity in the value chain?

  • A) Human Resources
  • B) Technology Development
  • C) Operations and Manufacturing
  • D) Procurement

Correct Answer: C

Operations and Manufacturing is a core activity involving the transformation of inputs into outputs. The others are support activities.

The Rise of Enterprise Systems

Historically, companies used Standalone Applications for specific departments (e.g., a separate system for Accounting and Warehouse).

The Problem: Silos

Standalone systems do not communicate. Information is re-entered manually, leading to errors, redundancy, and inefficiency.

These older, disconnected systems are often called Legacy Systems.

The Enterprise System Solution

An integrated suite of business applications for virtually every business process.

Key Features:

  • Central Database: One repository for all data.
  • Common Interface: Same look and feel across modules.
  • Data Sharing: Information entered in one module is immediately available to all others.

Supporting Business Processes

Internally Focused Systems

Support functional areas and decision making within the organization.

Value is added at each link in the internal chain.

Externally Focused Systems

Streamline communication with customers, suppliers, and partners outside the boundary.

Interorganizational Systems (IOS).

Software Types: Packaged vs. Custom

  • Packaged (Off-the-Shelf): Written by vendors for many users. Cost-effective but may not fit unique needs perfectly.
  • Custom Software: Designed exclusively for a specific organization. Expensive but tailored to unique requirements.

Modules

Enterprise systems are designed around modules (components) that replace legacy systems (e.g., a Finance module, an HR module).

Configuration and Customization

  • Vanilla Version: The standard features out of the box.
  • Customization: Changing the software code to fit local needs. Can be costly and hard to upgrade.
  • Best Practices: Industry-standard processes built into the software.

Organizations are often encouraged to adapt their processes to the software's "Best Practices" rather than customizing the software.

Business Process Management (BPM)

A systematic approach to improving business processes. Often accompanies ERP implementation.

Steps:

  1. Develop a vision.
  2. Identify critical processes.
  3. Understand and measure existing processes.
  4. Identify IT levers for improvement.
  5. Design and implement new processes.

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)

Applications that integrate business activities across departmental boundaries.

Evolution

Evolved from Material Requirements Planning (MRP) in manufacturing.

Key Term: "Enterprise" - serving the whole organization.

Leading vendors: SAP, Oracle, Microsoft, Sage, Infor.

ERP Components

ERP Core Components

Support internal activities:

  • Financial Management: Accounting, reporting, performance management, corporate governance.
  • Operations Management: Simplifies, standardizes, and automates logistics, production, and sales.
  • Human Resource Management: Recruitment, payroll, performance reviews, benefits.

Enabling Processes with ERP

ERP modules work together to support cross-functional processes.

Order-to-Cash

Sales module captures order -> Warehouse module allocates stock -> Finance module sends invoice.

Procure-to-Pay

Supply chain module creates PO -> Warehouse receives goods -> Finance pays invoice.

Knowledge Check

Which of the following is considered an ERP "Extended Component"?

  • A) Financial Management
  • B) Human Resource Management
  • C) Customer Relationship Management (CRM)
  • D) Operations Management

Correct Answer: C

CRM focuses on external activities (dealing with customers), making it an extended component. Financial, HR, and Operations are core internal components.

Achieving Enterprise System Success

ERP implementations are complex, costly, and risky. Success requires more than just technology.

Top Recommendations

  1. Secure Executive Sponsorship.
  2. Get Help from Outside Experts.
  3. Thoroughly Train Users.
  4. Take a Multidisciplinary Approach.
  5. Evolve the Implementation.

1. Secure Executive Sponsorship

The primary reason for failure is lack of top-level support.

  • Executives have the authority to distribute resources.
  • They can authorize necessary changes to business processes.
  • If executives don't prioritize the project, neither will the users.

Expertise and Training

Outside Experts

ERP systems are complex. Consultants help define requirements and train staff. But don't rely solely on vendor salespeople.

Train Users

Often overlooked and under-budgeted. Users must learn new software AND new business processes. Lack of training leads to productivity drops.

Costs of Enterprise Systems

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) includes much more than just the software license.

Underestimating these costs leads to budget overruns.

Security Matters: To Update or Not?

ERP systems often handle critical data. Keeping them updated is essential for security.

  • The Problem: Updates can break customizations.
  • The Risk: Running outdated software leaves the organization vulnerable to hackers (e.g., the Oracle vulnerability that allowed fraudulent payments).
  • Best Practice: Regularly apply patches and minimize customization to make updates easier.

Future Trends: Cloud ERP

Organizations are moving away from large, in-house on-premise systems.

Software as a Service (SaaS)

Cloud-based ERP allows for scalability, agility, and reduced capital costs. It is especially popular for small and mid-sized businesses.

Two-Tier ERP: Running a legacy on-premise system at HQ but nimble cloud ERPs at subsidiaries.

Future Trends: Mobile ERP

Accessing ERP data on the go.

  • Real-time Management: Manage the business from anywhere.
  • Service Quality: Remote workers have instant access to customer info.
  • Data Accuracy: Capture data in the field (e.g., using sensors or mobile forms) rather than re-keying it later.

Key Takeaways

  • Business processes (Order-to-Cash, Procure-to-Pay) cross functional boundaries.
  • Enterprise systems integrate data and processes into a single system, eliminating silos.
  • ERP systems have Core (internal) and Extended (external) components.
  • Successful implementation requires executive support, training, and following best practices.
  • The future of ERP is in the Cloud and Mobile access.
