The Need for High-Quality Information

Today's organizations operate in a global, highly competitive, and rapidly changing environment.

The Key to Success

Effective management relies on high-quality and timely information to support decision making.

This information must be extracted from:

  • Ever-increasing amounts of structured and unstructured data.
  • A variety of internal and external sources.

Managing in the Digital World: Intelligence Through Drones

Drone technology has progressed rapidly, enabling new forms of data collection and autonomous operation.

Swarm Technology

Companies like Verge Aero allow thousands of drones to fly in unison, deciding autonomously how to avoid collisions and reach destinations.

Pandemic Response

Drones were used to honor healthcare workers and can be used for contactless delivery of medical supplies.

Drones in Surveillance & Delivery

Surveillance

The U.S. Army and DARPA use tiny drones for real-time intelligence. Swarm technology allows them to map cities and navigate inside buildings without GPS.

Delivery

Amazon Prime Air aims to deliver packages within 30 minutes using small flying drones, envisioning a future where local warehouses dispatch automated fleets.

Drones in Agriculture

Drones gather geographic data to help farmers increase efficiency and precision.

Precision Agriculture

Current technology leverages sensors and AI to monitor crops, livestock, and soil quality. Drones can even deliver fertilizers to specific plots that need it most.

Disaster Recovery: Drones quickly assess damage from natural disasters like flooding, helping farmers recover faster.

Why Organizations Need Business Intelligence

Business processes often span multiple departments. The information needed to monitor them is often "missing"—hidden in disconnected spreadsheets and databases.

Data-Driven Organizations

Organizations that make decisions backed by verifiable data are measurably more productive and profitable. They can better plan for the future and respond to threats.

External Threats and Opportunities

The business environment is characterized by unstable market conditions and fierce competition.

  • Globalization: Provides new markets but also increases competition from developing countries.
  • Consumer Demands: Customers can easily switch to competitors; loyalty is harder to maintain.
  • Regulation: Stringent laws (e.g., Sarbanes-Oxley) require rigorous reporting and data management.

BI helps organizations respond swiftly to these pressures.

Big Data: Volume, Variety, Velocity

Volume: Unprecedented amounts of data.

Variety: Structured (databases), Semistructured (sensor data), Unstructured (social media, video).

Velocity: Rapid data generation and the need for real-time processing.

Big Data Opportunities

Analyzing Big Data can reveal hidden relationships and patterns.

Structured Data

Fits neatly into spreadsheets. Transaction data, customer records. Essential for operational efficiency.

Unstructured Data

Messy and complex. Text, audio, video, social sentiment. analyzing this is expensive but yields deeper insights.

Successful organizations leverage both to form a comprehensive strategy.

Databases: The Foundation of Intelligence

Database

A collection of related data organized in a way that facilitates data searches. They are vital to an organization's success.

Databases power everything from inventory tracking and sales transactions to the dynamic content on e-commerce websites.

Enabling Interactive Websites

E-commerce sites like Amazon rely on databases to provide dynamic content.

  • Product Catalogs: Data about products (name, price, image) is stored in a database and inserted into page templates.
  • Transactions: When you order, the system reads and writes to the database instantly.
  • Scale: Amazon processes millions of transactions daily, requiring highly efficient database management.

Database Terminology

Understanding the structure of a database is essential.

Entity (Table)

Something you collect data about (e.g., Customers, Products).

Record (Row)

A collection of related attributes about a single instance of an entity (e.g., one specific customer).

Attribute (Column)

Individual pieces of data (e.g., Name, SSN, Price).

Advantages of the Database Approach

Before DBMS, data was stored in redundant, flat files. The database approach offers:

  • Minimal Data Redundancy: Single copy of data saves space.
  • Improved Data Consistency: Changes (e.g., address update) apply everywhere.
  • Increased Security: Centralized access control.
  • Program-Data Independence: Changing software doesn't require rebuilding the data.

Database Types: RDBMS vs. NoSQL

RDBMS (Relational)

Balances efficiency and ease of retrieval.

Stores data in tables linked via relationships.

Good for structured data (e.g., accounting).

NoSQL

Highly scalable, distributed across many machines.

Flexible data types (images, audio, social posts).

Essential for Big Data and Cloud Computing (e.g., Amazon SimpleDB).

Effective Database Management

A database is only as good as the data it holds.

  • Data Model: A map or diagram that represents entities and their relationships.
  • Data Dictionary: Defines the format, type, and valid values for each attribute (metadata).
  • Business Rules: Policies that prevent illegal or illogical entries (e.g., a ship date cannot be before the order date).

Master Data Management (MDM)

Single Version of the Truth

It is imperative that marketing, accounting, and sales share the same definitions and data for "Customer" or "Revenue".

Master Data: The data deemed most important in the operation of a business (customers, suppliers, employees). MDM is a management issue, not just a technology issue.

Entering and Querying Data

Data is captured via Forms (e.g., web checkout, surveys) or automatic sensors (IoT, POS terminals).

To retrieve data, we use a Query.

SQL (Structured Query Language)

The most common language for interfacing with RDBMS.

SELECT AUTHOR, TITLE FROM BOOKS WHERE PRICE < 20

Common Reports & Queries

Business users need data presented in useful formats.

  • Scheduled Reports: Produced at predefined intervals (daily/weekly).
  • Exception Reports: Highlight situations out of the normal range.
  • Drill-down Reports: Provide greater detail to analyze root causes.
  • Ad Hoc Queries: Unplanned information requests for non-routine decisions.

OLTP vs. OLAP

OLTP (Operational)

Online Transaction Processing

Run the business on a current basis.

Fast processing of simple transactions (orders, payments).

Focus: Efficiency & Speed.

OLAP (Informational)

Online Analytical Processing

Support managerial decision making.

Complex queries on historical data.

Focus: Analysis & Insight.

Data Warehouses

Operational systems are optimized for transactions, not analysis. Running complex queries on them slows down the business.

Data Warehouse

A company's virtual storehouse of valuable data from disparate information systems and external sources. It supports analysis without impacting operational performance.

Used by companies like Walmart and UPS to integrate data for comprehensive analysis.

The ETL Process

Consolidating data from various systems into a warehouse.

Transformation includes data cleansing: detecting and correcting corrupt or inaccurate data.

Data Marts

A Data Warehouse can be massive and expensive (millions of dollars). A Data Mart is a data warehouse that is limited in scope.

  • Contains selected data for a specific end-user group (e.g., Marketing Data Mart, Inventory Data Mart).
  • Lower cost (<$1M) and easier to deploy.
  • Can be deployed on less powerful hardware.

BI vs. Advanced Analytics

Business Intelligence (BI)

Focus: What happened?

Analyzes past and current data.

Tools: Reports, Dashboards, OLAP.

User: Business Users.

Advanced Analytics

Focus: Why? What will happen?

Predictive modeling, pattern discovery.

Tools: Data Mining, Machine Learning.

User: Data Scientists/Analysts.

Decision Support Systems (DSS)

Interactive decision aids that use models to manipulate data.

  • What-if Analysis: Hypothetical changes to data (e.g., "What if interest rates rise by 1%?").
  • Goal-Seeking: Determining input parameters to achieve a desired result (e.g., "What sales volume is needed to reach $1M profit?").
  • Optimization: Finding the best balance between parameters within constraints.

OLAP: Dimensions and Measures

OLAP allows complex, multidimensional analysis.

  • Measures (Facts): The values or numbers to analyze (e.g., Sales Revenue, Profit).
  • Dimensions: Ways to summarize the data (e.g., Region, Time, Product Line).

Dimensions often have hierarchies (Year > Quarter > Month > Day).

The OLAP Cube

An OLAP cube allows for Slicing and Dicing.

Slicing: Analyzing a subset of the data (e.g., only Q2 sales).

Dicing: Analyzing a slice on another dimension (e.g., Q2 sales for the Western Region only).

Information Visualization: Digital Dashboards

Visualization: Displaying complex data relationships graphically.

Digital Dashboards

Provide a quick overview of Key Performance Indicators (KPIs). They use traffic lights, speedometers, and charts to highlight trends and exceptions.

Dashboards support: Push reporting (overview), Exception alerts (problems), and Pull reporting (drill-down).

Visual Analytics

Combining human intelligence with machine capability.

  • Problem: Data is often noisy and complex. Machines are fast but lack intuition.
  • Solution: Interactive visualizations that allow humans to spot patterns that algorithms might miss.
  • Example: Tracking hurricane intensification by visualizing "hot towers" in cloud data.

Visual Analytics in Action

Imagine trying to understand a hurricane from a spreadsheet of numbers. It's nearly impossible.

Visual analytics allows analysts to see the "story" in the data, such as the formation of a storm or a sudden shift in customer sentiment.

Knowledge Check

What is the primary difference between OLTP and OLAP?

  • A) OLTP is for managers; OLAP is for clerks.
  • B) OLTP is for current, operational transactions; OLAP is for analyzing historical data.
  • C) OLTP uses Data Warehouses; OLAP uses Data Marts.
  • D) OLTP is slower than OLAP.

Correct Answer: B

OLTP (Online Transaction Processing) is designed for fast, daily operations like processing orders. OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) is designed for complex analysis of historical data to support decision making.

Advanced Analytics: Data Mining

Data mining provides capabilities for discovering "hidden" predictive relationships in data.

  • Atheoretical: "Panning for gold" without a specific hypothesis.
  • Data Reduction: Essential preparatory step to handle massive datasets.
  • Algorithms: Search for patterns, trends, or rules.

Association Discovery

Technique used to find correlations among sets of items.

The "Coffee and Sugar" Rule

Mining sales data might reveal that 80% of people who buy coffee also buy sugar.

  • Support: % of transactions where both occur.
  • Confidence: Reliability of the correlation.

Sequence Discovery: Finding associations over time (e.g., buying a TV today leads to buying a Blu-ray player next month).

Clustering vs. Classification

Clustering (Unsupervised)

Grouping related records based on similarity.

The groups are not known beforehand.

Example: Finding natural customer segments based on spending habits.

Classification (Supervised)

Segmenting records into known classes.

The algorithm is trained on labeled data.

Example: Classifying loan applicants as "Low Risk" or "High Risk".

Text Mining

Analyzing unstructured text to extract information. 80-90% of data is unstructured.

  • Sources: Emails, call center logs, social media posts, wikis.
  • Sentiment Analysis: Learning about customers' thoughts, feelings, and emotions.
  • Application: Identifying fraud, monitoring employee satisfaction, competitive intelligence.

Web Content & Usage Mining

  • Web Content Mining: Extracting textual information from web pages using "web crawlers" or spiders. Used for competitive intelligence.
  • Web Usage Mining (Web Analytics): Analyzing clickstream data.
    • How do users navigate the site?
    • Stickiness: The ability to attract and keep visitors.
    • Optimizing website structure based on user paths.

Machine Learning & Neural Networks

Machine Learning: Algorithms that allow systems to learn patterns from data without being explicitly programmed.

Neural Networks

A network of processing elements that approximate the human brain. They learn by example.

Example: Training a network on thousands of past loan applications to recognize the patterns of a default.

Predictive Modeling

Moving from "What happened?" to "What will happen?"

  • Uses statistics, data mining, and machine learning to build explanatory models.
  • Application: Uber Surge Pricing.
  • Uber uses predictive models to forecast demand spikes in specific areas, adjusting prices proactively to balance supply (drivers) and demand.

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

The science of enabling information technologies to simulate human intelligence.

  • Capabilities: Reasoning, learning, seeing, hearing, feeling.
  • Real-world AI: Not just science fiction. It's in banking (fraud detection), medicine (diagnosis), and engineering.
  • Deep Learning: Advanced neural networks used for complex tasks like fighting "superbugs" (drug-resistant bacteria).

Intelligent Systems

Systems composed of sensors, software, and computers embedded in machines.

Robotics

collaborative robots work alongside humans. Drones monitor inventory in warehouses.

Sensor-Driven Computing

Decreasing sensor costs (e.g., accelerometers < $0.50) enable massive data collection for AI analysis.

Intelligent Agents (Bots)

Programs that work in the background to perform specific services.

  • User Agents: Perform tasks like filling forms or sending reports (e.g., Siri, Cortana).
  • Buyer Agents (Shopping Bots): Search the web for the best price.
  • Monitoring Agents: Track inventory levels or competitor prices.
  • Chatbots: Simulate human conversation for customer service.

Robotic Process Automation (RPA)

A technique to automate repetitive organizational tasks.

How it works

RPA monitors how a user performs a task (like copying data from an email to a spreadsheet) and learns to automate that execution. It frees up human workers for more high-value tasks.

Knowledge Management (KM)

Data and Information are not enough. We need Knowledge.

Data

Raw symbols (e.g., 45, 66).

Information

Formatted data (e.g., 45 items sold).

Knowledge

Understanding relationships, skills, and experience.

KM Systems: Processes to gain the greatest value from knowledge assets.

Tacit vs. Explicit Knowledge

Explicit Knowledge

Can be documented, archived, and codified. Stored in databases and manuals.

Tacit Knowledge

Stored in a person's mind. "Know-how," intuition, and experience. Hard to capture.

Best Practices: Effective procedures that are often tacit.

KM: Benefits and Challenges

Benefits

Enhanced innovation.

Improved customer service.

Employee retention.

Streamlined operations.

Challenges

Getting employee buy-in.

Dealing with knowledge overload.

Keeping knowledge current (obsolescence).

Social Network Analysis

Employees often work in "islands" of knowledge.

Connecting the Islands

Social network analysis maps contacts to discover connections or missing links (structural holes). It helps find experts and groups that should be collaborating but aren't.

Knowledge Portals: Ways to share this knowledge (e.g., Intranets for employees, Extranets for suppliers).

Geographic Information Systems (GIS)

A system for creating, storing, analyzing, and managing geographically referenced data.

  • Location Analytics: Adding a spatial dimension to data.
  • Example: Pairing population density and average income with a map to find the best location for a new restaurant.
  • Everyday Use: Google Maps, Uber (matching riders and drivers).

GIS Layers

GIS works by layering different types of data on a map.

Retail

Disaster Response

Evacuation planning, identifying flood zones, tracking disease spread.

Ethical Dilemma: The Orwellian IoT

IoT devices collect massive amounts of data: smart thermostats, fridges, even toilets.

  • The Fear: "Big Brother" surveillance. Data could end up with law enforcement, marketers, or hackers.
  • Deepfakes: AI can create realistic fake videos (e.g., faked CEO voice authorizing a transfer).
  • Question: How do we balance the convenience of smart devices with the right to privacy?

Industry Analysis: Healthcare

Big Data and AI are transforming healthcare.

  • Telemedicine: Remote diagnosis and surgery.
  • Quantified Self: Using wearables (Fitbit, Apple Watch) to track health data.
  • AI Diagnosis: Using visual analytics and machine learning to detect diseases in X-rays more accurately than humans.
  • Drug Discovery: Using Deep Learning to find new antibiotics for "superbugs".

Key Takeaways

  • Business Intelligence and Advanced Analytics are essential for surviving in a competitive, data-rich world.
  • Databases and Data Warehouses form the foundation for all analysis.
  • Visual Analytics combines human intuition with machine processing power.
  • Advanced Analytics (Mining, AI, ML) shifts focus from "what happened" to "what will happen" (predictive).
  • Knowledge Management and GIS provide specialized decision support for knowledge assets and spatial data.
