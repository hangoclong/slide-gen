Chapter Preview: Our Digital World

Technology's progress has been tremendous, leading to an exponential acceleration in the digital connection of people, things, and organizations. This is known as increasing digital density.

This chapter's objective is to help you understand the profound role of information systems (IS) in this new landscape, including their impact on societal issues, the future of innovation, and the ethical dilemmas that arise.

We will explore what information systems are, how they have become vital to modern organizations, and why this knowledge is essential for you as a future manager.

The Old Model of Innovation

Where did good ideas used to come from?

For decades, the stereotype of innovation was an eccentric inventor or a secretive corporate lab. This "closed" model had significant drawbacks:

  • High Cost & Slow Pace: Corporations funded their own expensive R&D operations, which took years to set up and were highly constrained.
  • Bureaucratic Hurdles: Research plans were evaluated by multiple layers of management, making the process slow and complex.
  • Market Disconnect: This bureaucracy often left research out of date and out of touch with actual customer wants and needs.

The result? A high risk of spending millions on products that were years late or no longer relevant, leading to market failure.

A New Approach: Open Innovation

Instead of relying on tightly controlled internal projects, companies are opening up their research and development efforts to a broad audience.

Open Innovation

This approach invites customers, suppliers, other companies, and universities to participate more directly in different phases of the innovation process. It's a shift from "we must invent it ourselves" to "let's find the best ideas, wherever they are."

This collaborative model is made possible by modern information systems that facilitate communication and idea sharing on a global scale.

Open Innovation in Action

My Starbucks Idea

Customers post ideas and suggestions, vote on them, and discuss them. Hundreds of customer-generated ideas have been implemented.

Heineken's Innovators Brewhouse

Uses open innovation to generate ideas on topics from counterfeit detection to new product packaging and market strategies.

Open innovation is also used to tackle major challenges, like fighting COVID-19, by linking experts across industries.

Information Systems Are Everywhere

Information systems (IS) are ubiquitous, woven into the fabric of modern business and society. We interact with them constantly, often without realizing the complexity behind the scenes.

Business Operations

Companies like FedEx and UPS use IS to route their entire fleet and track millions of packages in real-time. Retailers like Walmart use it to optimize global supply chains and analyze customer preferences.

Public Infrastructure

Cities use IS for adaptive traffic control to ease congestion. Modern cars are filled with information systems, controlling everything from the engine and airbags to park-assist and navigation features.

Innovative business models, from Airbnb to Uber, are built entirely on information systems.

IS in Your Daily Life

Your personal life is also powered by a vast array of information systems:

  • Education: You register for classes online, communicate with instructors via email, access e-books from the library, and submit assignments on platforms like Canvas or Blackboard.
  • Finance: Your paycheck is likely generated by a computer and automatically deposited into your bank account via high-speed networks.
  • Leisure: You use social networks like Facebook or TikTok to connect with friends, watch videos on YouTube, listen to music on Spotify, and use smartphone apps for everything from gaming to navigation.

Part 2: The Knowledge Society

From Industrial to Digital

The Emergence of the Digital World

Powerful, inexpensive, and easy-to-use computers have had a major impact on business and society.

We are now in the post-PC era, where connected mobile devices like tablets and smartphones are replacing traditional desktops for many tasks.

In the last quarter of 2011, Apple sold more iPads than HP sold PCs. This marked a significant shift in computing.

New Ways of Working and Socializing

Technology has untethered us from our desks. Work is no longer confined to a 9-to-5 schedule at a specific location.

  • Tasks like sending emails can be done anytime, anywhere.
  • The boundaries between work and leisure are blurring.
  • Cloud computing (Gmail, Dropbox) allows access to files and data from any device.

The Cycle of Change

We are in a virtuous cycle where technological changes lead to social changes, and social changes shape new technology.

graph LR A[Tech Changes] --> B[Social Changes]; B --> A; style A fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9,stroke-width:2px style B fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5,stroke-width:2px

For example, the demand for mobility and constant connectivity (a social change) drives the development of better smartphones and faster mobile networks (a technological change).

The Rise of the Knowledge Worker

In 1959, long before the internet as we know it, management guru Peter Drucker predicted that information and the people who worked with it would become increasingly important.

Knowledge Worker

Drucker coined this term to describe professionals who are relatively well educated and who create, modify, and/or synthesize knowledge as a fundamental part of their jobs.

His predictions were remarkably accurate, and knowledge workers are now a cornerstone of the modern economy.

Characteristics of Knowledge Workers

Drucker's predictions outlined a new type of employee with distinct traits:

  • Better Compensation: They are generally paid better than their prior agricultural and industrial counterparts.
  • Empowered by Education: They rely on formal education but also possess valuable real-world skills.
  • Continuous Learners: They are constantly learning how to do their jobs better and adapt to new challenges.
  • Greater Bargaining Power: Their skills and knowledge give them far more career opportunities and influence than workers of the past.

Knowledge workers now make up about a quarter of the workforce in developed nations, and their numbers are rising quickly.

The Knowledge Society

Drucker also predicted that the rise of knowledge workers would lead to a knowledge society.

He reasoned that education would become its cornerstone, and that possessing knowledge would be as important as possessing land, labor, or capital were in previous eras.

This concept is also known by other names, such as the knowledge economy, the new economy, the digital society, or the internet era. All these terms share the premise that information, technology, and knowledge workers are now vital to success.

In the knowledge society, knowledge has become the most critical resource.

The Economic Value of Education

Data from the U.S. Census Bureau shows a clear link between education and earnings.

Median annual earnings for workers 25 and over:

  • Graduate/Professional Degree: $66,944
  • Bachelor's Degree: $50,515
  • High School Diploma: $27,868
  • No High School Diploma: $19,954

The Next Evolution: The Learning Worker

Today, almost every worker can be considered a knowledge worker. The lines between "knowledge" and "manual" work are blurring.

The worker of the future needs to be a learning worker.

It's not just about what you know, but your ability to learn new things that will be of primary importance.

Part 3: Challenges in the Digital World

The Other Side of the Coin

The Digital Divide

While the digital world offers immense opportunity, it also creates significant challenges. One of the most critical is the digital divide.

Digital Divide

The gap between those who have access to information systems and the skills to use them, and those who do not. This creates a new class structure where power comes from knowledge, not just money or land.

This divide is a major ethical challenge, as it is strongly linked to a person's ability to compete and participate in the modern world.

Who is Affected by the Digital Divide?

While the gap is shrinking in some developed nations, major challenges remain. Groups that often lag behind national averages for internet access and computer literacy include:

  • People in rural communities.
  • The elderly.
  • People with disabilities.
  • Minority groups.

Outside of developed countries, the gap gets even wider. Many developing nations lack the modern infrastructure, financial resources, and affordable internet access needed to close the divide.

Part 4: Globalization & Societal Issues

A Connected Planet

What is Globalization?

Globalization refers to the integration of economies throughout the world, a trend enabled by innovation and technological progress. We can see its effects in many ways:

  • Greater international movement of commodities, money, information, and labor.
  • The development of global technologies, standards, and processes.
  • Falling transportation and telecommunication costs that connect the world.

The rapid rise of a new middle class in many developing countries has enabled companies to reach millions of new customers, dramatically expanding their markets.

Opportunities of Globalization

Access to New Markets

Companies can sell their products and services to literally millions of new customers in emerging economies.

Access to Global Talent

Decreasing communication costs allow companies to draw on a large pool of skilled professionals from all over the globe, from software developers in India to call centers in Ireland.

This has also led to the rise of outsourcing, where business processes are moved to other companies or countries to reduce costs and increase efficiency.

Focus on: Outsourcing

Outsourcing is the moving of business processes or tasks (such as accounting, manufacturing, or security) to another company or another country. Key reasons include:

  • To reduce or control costs.
  • To free up internal resources for core activities.
  • To gain access to world-class capabilities or skills.
  • To increase process efficiencies and reduce time to market.

However, outsourcing is not without its challenges. Cost savings can sometimes be negligible due to added overhead like customs, shipping, and training.

Challenges of Globalization

Operating on a global scale introduces significant operational and cultural hurdles:

Governmental

Dealing with differences in political systems, regulatory environments, laws, and standards across nations.

Geoeconomic

Navigating differences in infrastructure, demographics, welfare levels, and the expertise of the local workforce.

Cultural

Managing differences in languages, beliefs, attitudes, religions, and especially different viewpoints regarding intellectual property.

Major Societal Issues in the Digital World

The same forces driving globalization and technological progress have also created pressing societal issues that will shape our future.

These are often called "megatrends."

Societal Issue 1: Demographic Changes

This refers to changes in the structure of populations.

  • Aging Populations: Many developed countries have rapidly aging populations.
  • Population Growth: Developing regions like Africa are expected to see rapid population growth.
  • Migration: Mass migrations are affecting both developed and developing nations.

These shifts impact the supply and demand of labor and can increase disparities in welfare.

Societal Issue 2: Rapid Urbanization

Urbanization is the movement of rural populations to urban areas.

Over 50% of the world's population now lives in cities.

The major challenge is sustaining this growth while providing livable, safe, and sustainable environments for all inhabitants.

Societal Issue 3: Shifts in Economic Power

Established economies are losing their dominant positions in the world's economy.

  • The rise of new economic powers (e.g., China, India) is changing global dynamics.
  • This can lead to political struggles over resources and influence.

Societal Issue 4: Resource Scarcity & Climate Change

Population growth, global trade, and consumerism contribute to:

  • Increased waste and pollution.
  • A growing need for finite natural resources.
  • Climate Change: Large-scale changes in temperatures and weather patterns, leading to rising sea levels and more severe storms.

The Goal: Sustainable Development

In response to these challenges, sustainable development has become a critical goal.

Sustainable Development

"Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs."

- World Commission on Environment and Development, 1987

Part 5: The Future is Digital

Understanding Digital Density

Digital Density: The Engine of the Future

In most developed societies, information technologies have become pervasive, and the speed of innovation is increasing. This has led to an exponential increase in digital density.

Digital Density

The amount of connected data generated per unit of activity. As every action, from a purchase to a social media post, generates more connected data, it enables new value-added interactions and disruptive business models.

Understanding this concept is critical, as it impacts our private and work lives, and businesses must adapt their strategies to survive in the digital future.

The Drivers of Digital Density

The two essential drivers of digital density are the massive increase in Connections (through mobile devices and the IoT) and the explosion of Data (Big Data) they generate.

Driver 1: Connections

In the past, connections were between people or computers. Today, it is possible to connect almost any element of the physical world—people, organizations, or things—to the digital realm.

Key Enablers:

  • Mobile Devices: Smartphones and tablets provide constant, "instant-on" connectivity, blurring the lines between work and leisure and creating "micro-moments" for customer interaction.
  • The Internet of Things (IoT): A network of a broad range of physical objects that can automatically share data over the internet, from smart meters to wearable technology.

The Internet of Things (IoT) Explained

The IoT connects physical objects by embedding them with sensors, software, and wireless radios. Already in 2008, more devices were connected to the internet than people on Earth.

Smart Home

Technologies like the Nest Learning Thermostat enable remote monitoring and control of lighting, heating, and home appliances.

Wearable Technologies

Accessories like the Apple Watch or Fitbit incorporate sensors to record physiological and environmental data, from heart rate to altitude.

Industrial IoT (IIoT)

The use of IoT in manufacturing to improve efficiency, monitor supply chains, and enable mass customization of products.

Driver 2: Data (Big Data)

The ability to connect everything has generated tremendous amounts of Big Data.

Big Data

Extremely large and complex datasets, characterized by the "3 Vs": high Volume (amount), Variety (types), and Velocity (speed of collection and analysis).

By 2025, the world's data is estimated to grow to 175 zettabytes. That's 175 trillion gigabytes, or the equivalent of 350 billion 512GB iPads.

Driver 3: New Interactions & Business Models

Together, increasing connections and data enable new value-added interactions. For many modern organizations, value is created from data, not just a large workforce.

Old Economy (e.g., Ford)

~300,000 employees

Value from physical assets and manufacturing.

Digital World (e.g., Airbnb)

~5,000 employees

Value created from data and network effects.

Connected data allows companies to anticipate changes, coordinate resources, and personalize offerings in ways never before possible.

The API Economy

A key driver of continuous innovation and new business models has been the rise of APIs (Application Programming Interfaces).

What is an API?

An intermediary that provides a way for different components of software to interact and exchange data or functionality using common web protocols. They allow a service provider to make parts of its functionality or data available for others to use.

In 2018, API traffic accounted for 83% of all web traffic, fueling what is now called the "API Economy."

How APIs Work: An Analogy

Think of an API just like a power socket in your home.

  • The power socket is a standardized interface.
  • It allows you to receive a service (electricity) from a provider (the utility company).
  • You don't need to know how the electricity is generated or delivered; you just plug in your device and it works.

Similarly, a company like Lyft uses Google Maps' API to get mapping functionality without having to build it themselves, allowing them to focus on their core business.

Part 6: Understanding Information Systems

The Core Components

Information Systems Defined

Information System (IS)

The combination of people and information technology that create, collect, process, store, and distribute useful data.

Information Technology (IT) includes:

  • Hardware: Physical computer equipment, such as a computer, tablet, or printer, as well as components like a monitor or keyboard.
  • Software: A program or set of programs that tell the computer to perform certain tasks.
  • Telecommunications Networks: A group of two or more computer systems linked together with communications equipment.

Data, Information, and Knowledge

Data

Raw, unformatted symbols, such as characters and numbers (e.g., 465889727). Data has no meaning in and of themselves and are of little value until processed.

Information

Data that has been formatted, organized, or processed to make them useful. It is a representation of reality that helps answer questions (e.g., SSN: 465-88-9727).

Knowledge

The ability to understand information, form opinions, and make decisions or predictions. It is a body of rules or guidelines used to organize data for a task.

The Path from Data to Knowledge

graph LR A[Data] --> B[Information]; B --> C[Knowledge]; style A fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9,stroke-width:2px style B fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5,stroke-width:2px style C fill:#eef2ff,stroke:#4338ca,stroke-width:2px

Information systems are essential for transforming raw data into useful information. People then apply their knowledge to understand this information and make decisions.

The People Component: Builders, Managers, and Users

An information system is of little use without people. The IS field includes a vast collection of roles, all of which are in high demand:

  • Developers: Systems analysts who analyze business requirements, and software developers who code, test, and debug programs.
  • Maintainers: Database administrators who manage data, and IT auditors who ensure compliance with standards.
  • Managers: IT managers who oversee existing systems, and Chief Information Officers (CIOs) who guide strategic planning.
  • Users: That's you! Every employee, manager, and customer who interacts with the system is a crucial part of it.

The Organization: The Context of Information Systems

Information systems do not exist in a vacuum; they are built and used within the context of an organization to achieve specific goals.

Organizations use IS to:

  • Become more productive and profitable.
  • Gain a sustainable competitive advantage.
  • Reach more customers and expand into new markets.
  • Improve the quality of customer service.

This is true for all types of organizations—professional, social, religious, educational, and governmental.

The Dual Nature of Information Systems

Information systems are like a double-edged sword: they can be a powerful weapon for competitive advantage, but they also carry immense risk.

IS That Works: FedEx

FedEx uses extensive, interconnected information systems to coordinate its global delivery network of 490,000 employees and 180,000 vehicles. This allows them to process millions of packages with millisecond-level decisions, positioning them as a global leader.

IS Gone Awry: Zoom Outage

During the COVID-19 pandemic, a massive, unforeseen surge in users caused a Zoom outage. This single point of failure disrupted work, school, and even church services for millions, highlighting the risks of over-reliance on a single provider.

Part 7: Ethics in the Digital Age

Navigating the Moral Landscape

IS Ethics: The PAPA Framework

Computer ethics is used to describe the moral issues and standards of conduct as they pertain to the use of information systems. With the rise of companies like Google and Facebook, ethical concerns have moved to the forefront.

In 1986, Richard O. Mason identified four key ethical issues of the information age, known as the PAPA framework:

  • Privacy: What information must an individual reveal to others?
  • Accuracy: Who is responsible for the authenticity and fidelity of information?
  • Property: Who owns information and how can it be sold or exchanged?
  • Accessibility: What information does a person or organization have the right to obtain about others?

Information Privacy

Information privacy is concerned with what information an individual should have to reveal to others in the workplace or through transactions like online shopping.

The downside of the Information Age is that others may now have access to personal information you would prefer to keep private (e.g., SSNs, credit card numbers, medical histories).

The "Database of Intentions"

Using demographic and psychographic data, companies can piece together highly accurate profiles of their users. This aggregation of data about what you want, purchase, and like creates a holistic and valuable picture, raising concerns about manipulation and data breaches.

Intellectual Property

Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind that have commercial value. Digital technology makes it effortless to copy and distribute IP, often without permission or compensation.

Examples of IP Violations:

  • Downloading pirated movies or music from peer-to-peer networks.
  • Sharing software licensed to your school or workplace with friends or family.
  • Using a copyrighted photograph on your blog without permission from the creator.
  • Using 3D printers to create counterfeit physical goods from digital models.

Session Recap: Key Takeaways

  • We live in a digital world characterized by increasing digital density, globalization, and major societal challenges.
  • An Information System combines people, technology, and data to create value.
  • The future belongs to "learning workers" who can adapt to new technologies.
  • IS can be a powerful tool for competitive advantage, but it also carries significant risks.
  • Ethical issues, especially around privacy (PAPA), are critical to consider in the digital age.
