The Foundation of the Digital World

Just as a city relies on its physical infrastructure (roads, power, water), a modern digital business relies on its Information Systems (IS) Infrastructure.

This infrastructure is the backbone that supports all business processes, competitive strategy, and data management. Effectively managing it is a complex but critical activity in today's digital world.

This chapter explores the key components of this infrastructure and the managerial challenges of building and maintaining it.

Analogy: A City's Infrastructure

A city's infrastructure provides essential services, some visible, some invisible.

Visible Infrastructure

  • Streets & Highways
  • Schools & Hospitals
  • Parks

"Invisible" Infrastructure

  • Power & Water Lines
  • Sewage Systems
  • Communication Networks

A strong infrastructure makes a city more livable and attractive to businesses. The same is true for a company's IS infrastructure.

What is IS Infrastructure?

Businesses rely on an information systems infrastructure to support their operations, analytics, and strategy.

IS Infrastructure

The hardware, system software, storage, networking, and data centers that form the foundation for an organization's information systems.

No matter the specific system, the infrastructure provides three basic capabilities: processing, storage, and transmission of data.

Infrastructure vs. Architecture

These two terms are related but distinct.

IS Infrastructure

The broad, flexible set of technical capabilities available to the organization. It's the "what you have."

IS Architecture

The formal description of how specific technologies and systems are arranged to support specific business processes and strategy. It's the "how you use it."

A well-designed architecture leverages the infrastructure to achieve business goals.

The Goal: Business-IT Alignment

Business-IT alignment is the continuous process of adjusting business goals and the IS architecture to achieve business objectives.

This alignment is a dynamic cycle: business needs drive infrastructure changes, and new infrastructure capabilities enable new business opportunities.

Part 2: Core Components of IS Infrastructure

A Deeper Dive

Component 1: Hardware

Hardware refers to the physical computers that run the applications and databases necessary for processing transactions and analyzing data.

Organizations use different classes of computers to support various business processes.

Six General Classes of Computers:

  1. Supercomputer
  2. Mainframe
  3. Server
  4. Workstation
  5. Personal Computer
  6. Mobile Device

Hardware: The Heavy Lifters

Supercomputer

The most powerful and expensive computers, used for massive scientific problems (e.g., climate modeling, nuclear research). Not typically used by business organizations.

Mainframe

Large, central computing systems for major corporations. Optimized for high availability, security, and resource utilization. Used for mission-critical applications like transaction processing.

Hardware: Business & Personal Computing

Server

A computer on a network that provides services (files, printing, web pages) to other users. Optimized for many concurrent users.

Workstation

High-performance single-user computers for tasks like engineering, medical imaging, and graphic design. Optimized for visualization and 3D rendering.

Personal Computer (PC)

General-purpose computers for personal productivity and small business tasks.

Hardware: The Edge and Beyond

Mobile Devices

Smartphones and tablets that have become an integral part of an organization's IS infrastructure, enabling work from anywhere.

Embedded Systems

Specialized computers optimized for a specific task, from controlling traffic lights to playing MP3s. A key part of the Internet of Things (IoT).

Comparing Computer Types

This table summarizes the key characteristics of the different classes of computers used in organizations, from powerful supercomputers to handheld mobile devices.

Notice the vast differences in simultaneous users, cost, and memory, which dictate their typical use cases.

The Internet of Things (IoT)

The IoT is a network of physical objects embedded with sensors, software, and other technologies that connect and exchange data over the internet.

Key IoT Technologies:

  • Sensors: Detect changes in environmental conditions (temperature, pressure, motion).
  • Controllers (PLCs): Automate machines and processes.
  • Radio Frequency Identification (RFID): Uses electromagnetic energy to transmit data, replacing traditional barcodes.

Focus on: RFID

Radio Frequency Identification (RFID) is rapidly replacing the standard barcode for tracking products and assets.

Advantages over Barcodes:

  • Does not require line-of-sight scanning.
  • Can be read from a greater distance.
  • Can hold more data, which can be updated.
  • Readable regardless of position or visibility.

RFID tags can be passive (small, inexpensive, short-range) or active (larger, battery-powered, long-range).

  • A) Server

Workstations are specifically designed for demanding single-user tasks like engineering, medical imaging, or graphics design, featuring fast processors, large memory, and advanced video cards.

Component 2: System Software

Application software (like Word or Excel) cannot interact directly with hardware. It needs an intermediary.

System Software

The collection of programs that control the basic operations of computer hardware. The most prominent type is the Operating System (OS).

Examples: Windows, macOS, Linux, Android, iOS.

The Role of the Operating System

The OS acts as a coordinator between all the different parts of a computer system.

It provides a common layer, so applications don't have to be written for every single hardware model.

Component 3: Storage

Organizations need to reliably store and retrieve tremendous amounts of data. There are three distinct types of data based on purpose:

Operational Data

Data used for managing business processes, like processing transactions. Requires fast access.

Backup Data

Short-term copies of data, used to recover from a system-related disaster.

Archival Data

Long-term copies of data, often for compliance and reporting purposes. Access speed is less critical.

Choosing the Right Storage

Different data types call for different storage technologies:

  • Hard Drives (HDD) & Flash Storage (SSD): Offer high access speeds. Ideal for operational data and frequent backups where quick recovery is needed.
  • Magnetic Tapes: Very low cost and have a long shelf life (up to 30 years). Ideal for archival data where access speed is not a priority.

The choice of storage medium is a trade-off between speed, cost, and longevity.

Knowledge Check

Long-term copies of organizational data, often used for compliance and reporting, are known as:

  • A) Operational data
  • B) Backup data
  • C) Archival data
  • D) Volatile data

Correct Answer: C

Archival data is stored for long-term purposes, such as meeting legal compliance requirements (e.g., Sarbanes-Oxley Act). Access speed is less critical than low cost and durability, making magnetic tape a common choice.

Component 4: Networking

Individual pieces of technology have little value on their own. It is through networking—connecting them together—that business value is realized.

Computer networking is the sharing of data or services. It requires three things:

  1. A sender and a receiver.
  2. A pathway or transmission medium (e.g., cable, wireless).
  3. Rules or protocols governing communication.

Networking: Bandwidth

To transmit vast amounts of data in a timely manner, adequate bandwidth is needed.

Bandwidth

The transmission capacity of a computer or communications channel, measured in bits per second (bps). It represents how much data can be reliably transmitted in one second.

Downloading a 45-minute TV show (200 MB):

  • ~9 hours on a 56 Kbps modem.
  • ~6 minutes on a 1 Mbps DSL connection.
  • ~2 minutes on a 15 Mbps high-speed connection.

Network Models: Who Does What?

Client-Server Network

Servers and clients have defined roles. Servers provide services, and clients (like your PC) request services. This is the typical business model.

Peer-to-Peer (P2P) Network

Any computer (a "peer") may both request and provide services. All peers have equivalent capabilities. Common in small offices or for file-sharing (e.g., BitTorrent).

Types of Networks by Size

Personal Area Network (PAN)

Wireless communication between devices in a very small area (e.g., Bluetooth headset to phone). Under 10 meters.

Local Area Network (LAN)

Sharing of resources between users, typically within a single building (e.g., an office network). Can be wired or wireless (WLAN/Wi-Fi).

Wide Area Network (WAN)

Connects multiple LANs over a large physical distance, from a city to worldwide. The Internet is the largest WAN.

The Internet vs. The World Wide Web

These terms are often used interchangeably, but they are not the same thing.

The Internet: A large, worldwide collection of networks that use a common protocol (TCP/IP) to communicate. It is the infrastructure.

The World Wide Web: A system of interlinked documents on the internet. It is a service that runs on the internet, providing a graphical interface to access content.

Core Web Concepts

  • Web Page: A document containing content and hyperlinks.
  • HTML (Hypertext Markup Language): The standard method for specifying the structure and content of web pages.
  • HTTP (Hypertext Transfer Protocol): The protocol used by web servers to process user requests for pages.
  • URL (Uniform Resource Locator): A unique address used to identify and locate a particular web page.

Dissecting a URL

A URL has three main parts:

  1. Host Name: The particular web server that will respond (e.g., www, maps).
  2. Domain Name: Helps people recognize the company or person (e.g., google).
  3. Top-Level Domain (TLD): Indicates the type of organization (e.g., .com, .edu, .gov).

The URL may also include a path to a specific resource on the server.

IP Addresses: The Real Address

All domain names are associated with one or more Internet Protocol (IP) addresses.

An IP address is a unique numeric address that identifies a computer or device on the internet, allowing the network to route messages to the proper destination.

IPv4

32-bit address, written as four numbers separated by periods (e.g., 128.196.128.233). Running out of addresses.

IPv6

128-bit address, allowing for a vastly larger number of connected devices to support the IoT.

APIs: The Connective Tissue of the Web

Web protocols are not just for web pages. They are also the basis for Application Programming Interfaces (APIs).

API

An interface that allows different applications to communicate and exchange data or functionality. A service consumer (like the Uber app) can access services from a provider (like Google Maps) without needing to know how the service is built.

APIs are the foundation of the modern "API Economy," enabling seamless integration between different services.

The Deep Web vs. The Dark Web

Not all web content is indexed by search engines like Google.

The Deep Web

Parts of the web that cannot be indexed by conventional search engines. This includes private databases, content behind paywalls, and corporate intranets. Most of the web is the deep web.

The Dark Web

A small part of the deep web used for nefarious purposes (e.g., illegal trade). It is typically only accessible using specialized browsers (like Tor) that provide anonymity.

Intranets and Extranets

Organizations use web technologies for private communications as well.

  • Intranet: A private network, contained within an organization, that uses web technologies to share internal information. It is secured behind a firewall.
  • Extranet: A private part of the internet that is cordoned off from ordinary users, enabling two or more firms to use the internet to do business together securely (often using VPNs).

Component 5: Data Centers

To handle the massive volume of data, organizations need hundreds or thousands of servers. These are housed in data centers.

Data Center

A dedicated space for an organization's IS infrastructure components. They range from a single server room to entire buildings.

Key considerations for data centers include:

  • Power and cooling (a major expense).
  • Security and protection from natural disasters.
  • Connectivity and low latency.
  • Modularity for easy expansion.

Part 3: Key Drivers for Evolving the IS Infrastructure

The Challenge of Constant Change

Driver 1: Rapid Obsolescence

Technology evolves at a blistering pace. This creates a constant pressure to upgrade.

Moore's Law

An observation made by Intel co-founder Gordon Moore in 1965, who hypothesized that the number of transistors on a chip would double about every two years. This prediction has held remarkably true for decades, driving exponential growth in computing power.

This rapid advancement leads to shorter IT cycles (new devices every 6-12 months) and the challenge of planned obsolescence.

The Vicious Cycle of Upgrades

Hardware and software obsolescence are locked in a self-perpetuating cycle.

graph LR A[New, More Powerful Hardware] --> B(Enables New, More Powerful Software); B --> A; style A fill:#e0f2fe,stroke:#0ea5e9,stroke-width:2px style B fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5,stroke-width:2px

New software is developed to take advantage of new hardware capabilities. However, this new software often requires the new hardware to run, forcing users and organizations to upgrade both.

Driver 2: Big Data & Storage Needs

Organizations are collecting and analyzing vast amounts of data from websites, social media, and sensors to gain business intelligence.

This "data explosion" creates a relentless demand for:

  • More storage space.
  • More powerful computing hardware.
  • More advanced database management systems.
  • More network bandwidth to handle the data flow.

Driver 3: Demand Fluctuations

The demand for computing resources is often not constant. It fluctuates, creating a major challenge for infrastructure planning.

Up to 70% of organizations' IS infrastructures are utilized at only 20% of their capacity.

This leads to two undesirable states:

  • Having too few resources during peak times (e.g., holiday shopping season), leading to lost business.
  • Having too many idle resources most of the time, wasting money.

Driver 4: Increasing Energy Needs

More powerful hardware consumes more electricity and generates more heat, requiring more energy for cooling.

A single server rack in a data center can consume as much power as 10 homes.

For organizations with thousands of computers, rising energy costs are a major issue, impacting both the bottom line and the company's environmental footprint.

Driver 5: The Need for Agility

Modern organizations must innovate constantly to survive. This requires an agile, experimental approach, like the lean startup methodology.

This involves rapid cycles of building and testing "minimum viable products" (MVPs).

It makes little sense to invest heavily in new, permanent infrastructure for an experiment that might fail. Organizations need a flexible, temporary way to provision resources.

Knowledge Check

The observation that the number of transistors on a chip doubles approximately every two years is known as:

  • A) The Law of Agility
  • B) Moore's Law
  • C) The Law of Obsolescence
  • D) The Cloud Computing Law

Correct Answer: B

Moore's Law, predicted by Intel co-founder Gordon Moore, has been a key driver of the rapid increase in computing power and the corresponding cycle of hardware and software obsolescence.

Part 4: Cloud Computing & Modern Trends

Addressing Infrastructure Challenges

What is Cloud Computing?

Given the challenges of managing infrastructure, there has been a shift from building and maintaining IS to consuming it as a service.

Cloud Computing

A model for enabling ubiquitous, convenient, on-demand network access to a shared pool of configurable computing resources (e.g., networks, servers, storage, applications) that can be rapidly provisioned and released with minimal management effort.

It uses a utility computing model, where you "rent" resources and pay only for what you use.

Essential Cloud Characteristics

  • On-Demand Self-Service: Access resources as needed without human interaction.
  • Rapid Elasticity: Scale resources up or down almost instantaneously.
  • Broad Network Access: Access services from anywhere with an internet connection.
  • Resource Pooling: Providers serve multiple customers from a shared pool of resources.
  • Measured Service: Pay only for what you use, like a utility.

Cloud Service Models

Cloud services are offered in different "layers," depending on how much control the customer needs.

Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)

Provides basic processing, storage, and networking. The customer has the most control. (e.g., Amazon Web Services)

Platform as a Service (PaaS)

Provides a platform for customers to run their own applications. User controls the app, provider manages the platform. (e.g., Microsoft Azure)

Software as a Service (SaaS)

The customer uses applications provided by the vendor. Easiest to deploy, least control. (e.g., Gmail, Salesforce)

As you move from IaaS to SaaS, you manage less, and the provider manages more.

Cloud Types: Public vs. Private

Public Cloud

Services are provided by a third-party vendor (e.g., Amazon, Google) and can be used by any interested party on a pay-per-use basis. Offers massive scalability.

Private Cloud

An internal cloud, owned and operated by the organization itself. It provides self-service and utility computing but gives the organization full control over data and security.

Many organizations use a hybrid cloud approach, combining public and private clouds for different needs.

Managing the Cloud: Key Issues

Moving to the cloud is not without risks. Management must consider several strategic issues:

  • Availability & Reliability: What happens if the provider has an outage?
  • Scalability: Can the provider meet future business needs?
  • Viability: What if the provider goes out of business?
  • Security, Privacy & Compliance: How is sensitive data protected? Who has access?
  • Openness: How difficult is it to move to another provider (vendor lock-in)?
  • Cost: Is it truly cheaper than an in-house infrastructure in the long run?

Advanced Trend: Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA)

To achieve greater agility, organizations are moving away from large, monolithic applications toward a Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA).

SOA

An approach where business processes are broken down into individual, reusable "services" (like "check inventory" or "process credit card"). These services can be combined like building blocks to create flexible applications.

APIs are the key technology used to connect these services.

Advanced Trend: Grid Computing

For problems too large for even a supercomputer, organizations can use grid computing.

Grid Computing

Combining the computing power of a large number of smaller, independent, networked computers into a cohesive system to solve a single large problem.

Large tasks are broken into small chunks, which are distributed to computers on the grid to be solved in parallel. Example: Folding@Home for disease research.

Advanced Trend: Edge Computing

To reduce latency, processing is moving from centralized data centers to the "edges" of the network, closer to the user.

Content Delivery Network (CDN)

A network of servers in various geographic locations that store copies of website content. The server closest to the user delivers the content, speeding up access.

Edge Computing

An evolution of CDNs where not just data, but also processing, is moved to the edge. This is crucial for low-latency applications like IoT, autonomous vehicles, and real-time analytics.

Advanced Trend: IP Convergence

IP Convergence is the use of the Internet Protocol (IP) for transporting voice, video, fax, and data traffic over a single, shared network.

This has enabled new forms of communication and collaboration at much lower costs.

  • Voice over IP (VoIP): Placing telephone calls over the internet (e.g., Skype).
  • Videoconferencing over IP: Transmitting video data for meetings over the internet (e.g., Zoom, Google Meet).

Advanced Trend: Green Computing

Rising energy costs and environmental concerns have made green computing a major focus.

Green Computing

The practice of using computing resources more efficiently to reduce environmental impacts and energy consumption.

Key Practices:

  • Using virtualization and cloud computing to consolidate servers.
  • Installing power management software.
  • Reducing paper waste.
  • Properly recycling or refurbishing obsolete hardware.

Session Recap: Key Takeaways

  • The IS infrastructure (hardware, software, storage, networking, data centers) is the foundation of the digital business.
  • Managing this infrastructure is challenging due to rapid obsolescence, demand fluctuations, and rising energy costs.
  • Cloud computing offers a flexible, scalable, and cost-effective solution by providing infrastructure as a utility.
  • Cloud models (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS) offer different levels of control and are chosen based on business needs.
  • Advanced trends like SOA, Edge Computing, and IP Convergence are further transforming how we build and use systems.
