The Vulnerability of Digital Dependence

As individuals and organizations become more dependent on digital technologies, the risks associated with security failures increase exponentially.

The Reality

We have never been more vulnerable to catastrophic security disasters. A single breach can compromise millions of records, financial data, and even physical infrastructure.

Managing in the digital world requires a proactive and comprehensive approach to Information Systems (IS) security.

What is Computer Crime?

Computer crime is broadly defined as the use of a computer to commit an illegal act.

Targeting a Computer

Gaining unauthorized access to cause damage or steal data (e.g., hacking into a bank server).

Using a Computer

Using a computer as a tool to commit an offense (e.g., stealing credit card numbers online, embezzlement).

Supporting Crime

Using computers to support criminal activity (e.g., drug dealers keeping records, money laundering).

Hacking vs. Cracking

Hackers (White Hats): Originally referred to experts motivated by curiosity and the desire to learn. They often help organizations find vulnerabilities (bug bounties).

Crackers (Black Hats): Individuals who break into computer systems with the intention of doing damage or committing a crime.

Note: The media often uses the term "hacker" for both, but the distinction is important in the security community.

Hacktivists: Electronic Vandals or Activists?

Hacktivists are computer criminals who attempt to break into systems or deface websites to promote political or ideological goals.

  • Goals: Free speech, human rights, anti-war campaigns, environmental justice.
  • Tactics: Website defacement, Denial of Service (DoS) attacks, leaking confidential information.

Example: Anonymous

A loose collective of hacktivists known for attacks on governments and corporations they perceive as acting unethically (e.g., Operation Payback, attacks on ISIS).

Who Commits Computer Crimes?

The profile of a computer criminal has evolved beyond the stereotype of a lone teenager.

  1. Current or Former Employees: Insider threats are the most common and often most damaging source of crime.
  2. People with Technical Knowledge: Committing sabotage for personal gain.
  3. Career Criminals: Using computers to assist in fraud, theft, or drug trafficking.
  4. Outside Crackers: Snooping or hoping to find valuable data (only ~10% cause damage).

Broad Categories of Computer Crimes

Theft & Fraud

Stealing money, merchandise, or time. Identity theft and financial fraud are major components.

Data Theft & Modification

Stealing proprietary information (Industrial Espionage) or altering records (e.g., changing grades, giving unearned raises).

Destruction

Electronic vandalism, planting viruses, crashing systems, and destroying data.

Unauthorized Access

This occurs whenever people who are not authorized to see, manipulate, or handle data gain access to it.

Methods of Access:

  • Technology: Using vulnerabilities scanners, packet sniffers (to intercept network traffic), and keyloggers (to capture keystrokes/passwords).
  • Social Engineering: Misrepresenting oneself to trick others into revealing information.
  • Shoulder Surfing: Looking over someone's shoulder while they type credentials.
  • Dumpster Diving: Scouring trash for passwords or sensitive info.

Insider Threats

"Trusted adversaries" operating within an organization's boundaries are often more dangerous than outside hackers.

Why are they dangerous?

They already have legitimate access to systems and know where the valuable data is stored. They can bypass many perimeter defenses (like firewalls).

Edward Snowden is a famous example of an insider who leaked classified documents from the NSA.

Malware: Computer Viruses

Malware is short for malicious software.

Virus

A destructive program that disrupts the normal functioning of information systems. Key characteristic: It can reproduce itself but needs a host file.

Viruses infect a single computer and spread when users share infected files (email attachments, USB drives). They can erase files, slow down processing, or destroy data.

Malware: Worms

A Worm is a variation of a virus that is targeted at networks.

Virus

Needs a host file (like a Word doc) and human action (sharing the file) to spread.

Worm

Independent program. Spreads by itself across networks using security holes. Can replicate endlessly and crash servers.

Trojan Horses and Logic Bombs

Trojan Horse

A program that appears legitimate and benign but carries a destructive payload. Unlike viruses, they typically do not replicate. They often open "backdoors" for attackers.

Logic Bomb / Time Bomb

A variation of a Trojan that lies in wait for a specific triggering operation (Logic) or date (Time).
Example: A disgruntled employee plants code to delete payroll records if his name is removed from the employee database.

Ransomware: The Growing Threat

Ransomware holds a user's computer or data hostage by encrypting files or locking the system.

1
User accidentally downloads ransomware (via email or website).
2
Malware encrypts critical files, making them inaccessible.
3
A message demands payment (usually Bitcoin) to receive the decryption key.

Having secure, offline backups is the best defense against ransomware.

Denial of Service (DoS) Attacks

Intruders attempt to prevent legitimate users from accessing a service.

Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)

Attackers use a Botnet (army of zombie computers) to flood a target server with requests. The server becomes overwhelmed and crashes, denying service to legitimate customers.

Zombie Computers: Computers infected with malware that allows an attacker to control them remotely without the owner's knowledge.

Cybercrime is often a sophisticated global business with specialized roles.

Spyware and Adware

Spyware: Software that covertly gathers data about a user without their knowledge. It can track web activity or capture keystrokes.

Adware: Free software paid for by advertisements. Sometimes contains spyware to customize ads.

Spyware eats up system resources, bandwidth, and exposes users to identity theft.

Spam and Phishing

Spam: Electronic junk mail. A nuisance that eats up storage and bandwidth. Often used to spread malware.

Phishing: Attempts to trick users into giving away authentication credentials by masquerading as a legitimate entity (e.g., a fake email from "Bank of America").

Spear Phishing

A sophisticated variation that targets a specific person or organization by personalizing the message to make it appear more trustworthy.

Knowledge Check

Which type of malware spreads by itself across networks without needing a host file?

  • A) Virus
  • B) Trojan Horse
  • C) Worm
  • D) Logic Bomb

Correct Answer: C) Worm

A worm is designed to spread by itself across networks, taking advantage of security holes, and does not need to attach to a host file like a virus does.

Identity Theft

One of the fastest-growing cybercrimes.

Definition: Stealing another person's Social Security number, credit card number, and other personal data for the purpose of using the victim's credit rating to borrow money, buy merchandise, or run up debts.

Solution: Government and private sector must work together to change methods of identity verification (e.g., moving beyond SSNs to biometrics).

Cyberharassment, Stalking, and Bullying

The anonymity of the internet enables targeted abuse.

  • Cyberharassment: Using a computer to communicate obscene, vulgar, or threatening content.
  • Cyberstalking: Intentionally following, threatening, and intimidating someone electronically.
  • Cyberbullying: Deliberately causing emotional distress to a victim, often by manipulating or discrediting them.
  • Doxing: Researching and broadcasting personally identifiable information about a victim.

Software Piracy

Software Piracy: The illegal practice of buying one copy of software and making multiple copies for others or selling them.

Global Impact

Costs the industry billions of dollars. There is a strong correlation between unlicensed software and malware infection rates.

Intellectual Property (Patents, Copyrights) laws protect software, but enforcement varies globally due to cultural differences regarding ownership of knowledge.

Cybersquatting

The practice of registering a domain name and then trying to sell it for a large sum to the person, company, or organization most likely to want it.

Example: Registering panasonic.com before Panasonic could, hoping they will pay to acquire it.

The Anti-Cybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (1999) makes it a crime to profit from the goodwill of a trademark belonging to someone else.

Laws Against Computer Crime

Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (1986)

Prohibits stealing data, gaining unauthorized access to government/bank computers, and threatening to damage systems.

Electronic Communications Privacy Act (1986)

Regulates the interception of electronic communications and wiretapping.

Challenges: Defining "damage," enforcing laws across international borders, and balancing surveillance with privacy.

Cyberwar

An organized attempt by a country's military to disrupt or destroy the information and communication systems of another country.

Patriot Hackers

Independent citizens who perpetrate attacks on perceived enemies of their country, often giving governments plausible deniability.

Cyberwar can blind, jam, or deceive enemy systems, targeting command-and-control, intelligence, and smart weapons.

Stuxnet: A Case Study in Cyberwar

Discovered in 2010, Stuxnet was a computer worm designed to infect specific industrial hardware inside Iranian nuclear plants.

  • Target: Centrifuges used for nuclear enrichment.
  • Effect: Manipulated motor speeds to destroy the equipment physically while telling the monitoring systems everything was normal.
  • Significance: The first publicly known cyberweapon to cause physical damage to critical infrastructure.

Cyberterrorism

The use of computer and networking technologies to intimidate or coerce governments, civilians, or any segment of society to attain political, religious, or ideological goals.

Advantages for Terrorists

Can be launched from anywhere. No borders to cross. Potential to disrupt critical infrastructure (power, water, finance).

Disadvantages

Systems are complex and hard to control. Requires high technical expertise. Lacks the "drama" of physical attacks.

Knowledge Check

What is the primary difference between Cyberwar and Cyberterrorism?

  • A) The tools used (viruses vs worms)
  • B) The perpetrator (Government Military vs Non-state Actors)
  • C) The target (Military vs Civilian)
  • D) There is no difference

Correct Answer: B

Cyberwar is organized by a country's military. Cyberterrorism is launched by individuals or organized groups (non-state actors) to cause fear and panic.

Managing Information Systems Security

IS Security refers to precautions taken to keep all aspects of information systems safe from destruction, manipulation, or unauthorized use.

It is an ongoing process, not a one-time fix.

Step 1: Assessing Risks

Organizations must identify and rank risks to determine effective strategies.

  • Threats: Undesirable events that can cause harm (e.g., hackers, floods).
  • Vulnerabilities: Weaknesses in the system that can be exploited (e.g., unpatched software, weak passwords).
  • Impact: The severity of the consequences if a threat exploits a vulnerability.

Protecting an asset only makes sense if the cost of protection is less than the probable loss.

Risk Assessment Matrix

Organizations typically rate risks based on Impact and Likelihood.

Risk Category Impact (1-3) Likelihood (1-3) Risk Level
Confidentiality Breach 3 (High) 2 (Med) 6 (High)
Data Integrity Loss 2 (Med) 3 (High) 6 (High)
System Availability 3 (High) 1 (Low) 3 (Med)

Options for Addressing Risk

Risk Reduction

Taking active countermeasures to protect systems (e.g., installing firewalls).

Risk Acceptance

Implementing no countermeasures and absorbing any damages (best for low likelihood/impact risks).

Risk Transference

Having someone else absorb the risk (e.g., insurance, outsourcing to cloud).

Risk Avoidance

Not performing the task that causes risk (e.g., not using email). Often impractical.

Step 2: Developing a Security Strategy

The strategy details what IS controls (technology, people, policies) should be implemented.

  • Preventive Controls: Stop the event from occurring (e.g., locks, passwords).
  • Detective Controls: Assess if anything went wrong (e.g., audit logs, intrusion detection).
  • Corrective Controls: Mitigate impact after an event (e.g., backups, disaster recovery).

Principle of Least Privilege

Users should only be given access to the systems, data, or resources that are needed to perform their duties.

Policies and Procedures

Non-technical controls are critical.

  • Acceptable Use Policy (AUP): Outlines appropriate use of company computers (e.g., restricting personal web surfing).
  • Confidential Information Policy: How sensitive data is handled.
  • Backup Policy: Requirements for data backup and restoration.
  • Disaster Recovery Plan: Steps to restore operations after a catastrophe.

Disaster Recovery Planning

Business Continuity Plan: Describes how business continues operating after a disaster.

Cold Backup Site

An empty warehouse with power/connections but no equipment. Cheap, but slow recovery.

Hot Backup Site

A fully equipped facility with data replication. Expensive, but fast recovery.

RTO (Recovery Time Objective): Max time allowed to recover.
RPO (Recovery Point Objective): How current the backup data must be.

Step 3: Implementing Controls

Physical Access Restrictions

The first line of defense is keeping unauthorized people away from hardware.

  • Identification: User claims an identity (Username).
  • Authentication: Confirming the identity (Password).
  • Authorization: Granting access to specific resources.

Types of Authentication

Authorization is granted based on:

  1. Something You Have: Keys, ID cards, Smart cards.
  2. Something You Know: Passwords, PINs, Secret questions.
  3. Something You Are: Biometrics (Fingerprints, Retina scans, Facial recognition).

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Using two types of credentials (e.g., a Password + a Code sent to your phone) significantly increases security.

Firewalls

A system designed to detect intrusion and block unauthorized traffic from entering a private network.

  • Acts as a security fence around the organization's perimeter.
  • Can be hardware, software, or both.
  • Filters data packets based on security criteria.

Encryption

The process of encoding messages so that only the intended recipient can read them.

Symmetric Encryption: Sender and receiver use the same key. (Challenge: Sharing the key securely).

Asymmetric (Public Key) Encryption: Uses a pair of keys (Public and Private). A message encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the private key.

How Public Key Encryption Works

graph LR A[Sender] -->|Encrypts with| B(Recipient's Public Key); B --> C[Encrypted Message]; C -->|Sent over Internet| D[Recipient]; D -->|Decrypts with| E(Recipient's Private Key); E --> F[Readable Message]; style B fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5 style E fill:#dbeafe,stroke:#4f46e5 style C fill:#e0f2fe

This allows for secure communication without pre-sharing a secret key.

Human Controls

Technology alone cannot secure a system. Human safeguards are essential.

  • Ethics: Promoting appropriate conduct.
  • Laws: Enforcing penalties for misuse.
  • Management: Oversight and proper resource allocation.
  • Training: Educating employees about phishing, password hygiene, and security policies.

Step 4: Monitoring Security

Organizations must continuously monitor controls to minimize risk.

  • Audit Trails: Records showing who accessed a system and what they did.
  • IS Auditing: External reviews of controls (often required by laws like Sarbanes-Oxley).
  • Sarbanes-Oxley (S-OX): Mandates companies to establish internal controls and preserve evidence (like emails) to prevent financial fraud.

Computer Forensics

The use of formal investigative techniques to evaluate digital information for judicial review.

  • Restoring deleted data.
  • Tracing illegal activity.
  • Used by law enforcement and organizations.

Honeypot

A computer or network site designed to entice crackers so their illegal activity can be detected and studied.

Knowledge Check

Which of the following is a form of biometric authentication?

  • A) A Password
  • B) A Smart Card
  • C) A Fingerprint Scan
  • D) A User ID

Correct Answer: C

Biometrics authorize users based on "Something You Are," such as fingerprints, retinal patterns, or facial features.

Key Takeaways

  • Computer crime takes many forms, from insider theft to global cyberwarfare.
  • Malware (Viruses, Worms, Ransomware) poses a constant threat to data integrity and availability.
  • Security is a management issue, not just a technology issue. It requires a cycle of Assessing, Developing, Implementing, and Monitoring.
  • A Defense-in-Depth strategy uses layers of controls: Physical, Technical (Firewalls, Encryption), and Human (Training, Policy).
